## 1. Purpose

This document defines how NervLock must identify, evaluate, synthesize, communicate, and update medical and scientific evidence.

It applies to all NervLock work involving:

- Brain health
- Neurology
- Cognitive health
- Healthy aging
- Sleep
- Stress
- Exercise
- Nutrition
- Metabolic health
- Lifestyle medicine
- Functional medicine
- Supplements
- Ingredients
- Diagnostic tests
- Clinical interventions
- Risk factors
- Biological mechanisms
- Public medical education
- Product rationale
- Product claims
- Patient and caregiver education

The purpose of this standard is to ensure that NervLock:

- Distinguishes strong evidence from weak evidence
- Does not confuse biological plausibility with clinical benefit
- Does not overstate preliminary research
- Communicates uncertainty proportionately
- Evaluates harms as well as benefits
- Uses evidence appropriate to the question being asked
- Protects readers from misleading interpretation
- Separates scientific evidence from commercial interest

Scientific evidence must be evaluated before marketing language is written.

---

## 2. Governing Principle

NervLock follows one central scientific rule:

> Confidence in language must never exceed confidence in evidence.

The strength of a conclusion must reflect:

- The study question
- Study design
- Risk of bias
- Consistency
- Directness
- Precision
- Effect size
- Clinical relevance
- Applicability
- Safety
- Publication bias
- Totality of evidence

No study type, journal reputation, author credential, or statistical result automatically establishes truth.

## 4. Evidence Review Is Question-Specific

Evidence must be evaluated according to the question being asked.

A study may be useful for one question and inadequate for another.

### 4.1 Intervention Question

Example:

> Does aerobic exercise improve cognitive function in adults with mild cognitive impairment?

Preferred structure:

- Population
- Intervention
- Comparator
- Outcome
- Time frame
- Setting

Use a PICO framework when appropriate.

### 4.2 Exposure or Risk-Factor Question

Example:

> Is chronic sleep deprivation associated with increased risk of cognitive decline?

Preferred structure:

- Population
- Exposure
- Comparator
- Outcome
- Time frame

Use a PECO framework when appropriate.

### 4.3 Diagnostic Question

Example:

> How accurately does a blood biomarker identify Alzheimer’s disease pathology in the intended clinical population?

Define:

- Intended population
- Index test
- Reference standard
- Target condition
- Clinical setting
- Intended use
- Decision threshold

### 4.4 Prognostic Question

Example:

> Does REM sleep behavior disorder predict future development of a synucleinopathy?

Define:

- Population
- Prognostic factor
- Outcome
- Follow-up period
- Competing risks
- Clinical setting

### 4.5 Mechanistic Question

Example:

> Through which biological pathways might exercise affect neuroplasticity?

Mechanistic evidence may explain plausibility.

It does not independently establish clinical benefit.

### 4.6 Safety Question

Example:

> What adverse effects or interactions may occur with this ingredient at the proposed dose?

Safety questions require active evaluation of:

- Adverse events
- Serious adverse events
- Discontinuations
- Drug interactions
- Dose dependence
- Vulnerable populations
- Duration of exposure
- Post-market signals

## 5. Match the Study Design to the Question

There is no single universal evidence hierarchy for every medical question.

The appropriate design depends on the question.

### Intervention Effects

Preferred evidence may include:

- High-quality systematic reviews of randomized trials
- Well-conducted randomized controlled trials
- Pragmatic randomized trials
- Carefully conducted non-randomized comparative studies when randomization is not feasible

### Harm and Long-Term Safety

Important evidence may include:

- Randomized trials
- Large cohort studies
- Case-control studies
- Registries
- Pharmacovigilance data
- Post-market surveillance
- Case series for rare signals

Randomized trials may be underpowered or too short to identify uncommon or delayed harms.

### Prognosis and Risk Factors

Preferred evidence may include:

- Prospective cohort studies
- Well-designed longitudinal studies
- Validated prognostic models
- Replication across independent populations

Randomized trials are not usually the primary design for many prognostic questions.

### Diagnostic Accuracy

Preferred evidence may include:

- Prospective or appropriately assembled diagnostic accuracy studies
- Representative patient populations
- Valid reference standards
- Prespecified thresholds
- Independent interpretation of tests
- External validation

### Disease Frequency

Preferred evidence may include:

- Population-based surveys
- Registries
- Representative cohort studies
- Administrative datasets with validated definitions

### Patient Experience and Acceptability

Useful evidence may include:

- Qualitative research
- Mixed-methods studies
- Surveys using validated instruments
- Patient preference studies

### Biological Mechanism

Useful evidence may include:

- Human experimental studies
- Biomarker studies
- Imaging studies
- Translational studies
- Animal models
- Cellular studies

Mechanistic evidence must not be treated as equivalent to patient-important clinical evidence.

## 6. Evidence Sources and Their Roles

### 6.1 Clinical Practice Guidelines

Guidelines may provide high-level clinical recommendations.

However, a guideline must be evaluated for:

- Issuing organization
- Methodology
- Evidence search process
- Evidence-to-decision process
- Conflict-of-interest management
- Date of publication
- Update status
- Target population
- Healthcare setting
- Recommendation strength
- Certainty of supporting evidence

A guideline is not automatically authoritative merely because it is called a guideline.

Consensus documents, position statements, and expert statements must not be treated as equivalent to methodologically rigorous guidelines.

### 6.2 Systematic Reviews

A systematic review may provide a comprehensive synthesis.

It must be evaluated for:

- Prespecified question
- Protocol registration
- Search completeness
- Study selection
- Duplicate screening
- Risk-of-bias assessment
- Appropriate synthesis
- Heterogeneity
- Publication bias
- Certainty assessment
- Currency of the search
- Conflicts of interest

A systematic review is not automatically high-certainty evidence.

### 6.3 Meta-Analyses

A meta-analysis is a statistical method, not a guarantee of evidence quality.

Its reliability depends on:

- Quality of included studies
- Compatibility of populations
- Compatibility of interventions
- Compatibility of outcomes
- Risk of bias
- Heterogeneity
- Model choice
- Small-study effects
- Publication bias
- Selective reporting

Combining multiple weak studies does not automatically create strong evidence.

### 6.4 Randomized Controlled Trials

Randomization can reduce confounding, but an RCT may still be unreliable because of:

- Poor allocation concealment
- Lack of blinding where blinding is important
- Differential attrition
- Non-adherence
- Missing data
- Selective outcome reporting
- Protocol deviations
- Small sample size
- Short duration
- Surrogate outcomes
- Industry influence
- Inappropriate analysis

### 6.5 Observational Studies

Observational studies are important for:

- Prognosis
- Risk factors
- Long-term outcomes
- Rare harms
- Real-world practice
- Populations excluded from trials

They require careful assessment of:

- Confounding
- Selection bias
- Exposure measurement
- Outcome measurement
- Reverse causation
- Time-related bias
- Missing data
- Model overadjustment
- Residual confounding

Association must not be presented as causation without appropriate justification.

### 6.6 Case Reports and Case Series

Case reports may:

- Identify rare adverse events
- Generate hypotheses
- Describe unusual clinical presentations
- Identify new signals

They cannot reliably establish:

- Frequency
- Comparative effectiveness
- Causality
- Generalizable treatment benefit

### 6.7 Expert Opinion

Expert opinion may help:

- Interpret incomplete evidence
- Explain clinical context
- Identify implementation issues
- Clarify uncertainty

Expert opinion must be labeled as interpretation.

Professional status does not convert opinion into evidence.

### 6.8 Narrative Reviews

Narrative reviews may provide context and explanation.

They may be vulnerable to:

- Selective citation
- Unclear search methods
- Author preference
- Incomplete evidence coverage

They should not be the sole basis for high-stakes claims when systematic evidence is available.

### 6.9 Preprints

Preprints have not completed formal peer review.

They may be used for emerging topics only when:

- Clearly labeled as preprints
- Methods are critically appraised
- Findings are not presented as established
- Publication status is checked before final use
- High-risk decisions do not rely solely on them

### 6.10 Conference Abstracts

Conference abstracts generally contain limited methodological detail.

They should not support strong clinical or product claims without a complete and verifiable report.

### 6.11 Press Releases and News Reports

Press releases and news reports are discovery tools, not primary evidence.

They must not be cited as proof of clinical benefit when primary evidence is available.

## 7. NervLock Certainty-of-Evidence Framework

NervLock uses four primary certainty levels.

These levels reflect confidence that the estimated effect or conclusion is sufficiently close to the truth for the stated purpose.

### High Certainty

NervLock has high confidence that the estimate or conclusion is close to the true effect.

Typical characteristics may include:

- Multiple well-conducted studies
- Low risk of bias
- Consistent findings
- Direct population and intervention
- Adequate precision
- Clinically relevant outcomes
- Low concern for publication bias
- Support from rigorous guidelines where applicable

High certainty does not mean absolute certainty.

### Moderate Certainty

NervLock has moderate confidence in the estimate or conclusion.

The true effect may be meaningfully different.

Possible limitations include:

- Some risk-of-bias concerns
- Limited number of studies
- Moderate imprecision
- Minor inconsistency
- Some indirectness
- Limited duration
- Incomplete replication

### Low Certainty

Confidence is limited.

The true effect may be substantially different from the estimate.

Common reasons include:

- Serious risk of bias
- Small studies
- Inconsistent findings
- Indirect evidence
- Wide confidence intervals
- Surrogate outcomes
- Short follow-up
- Significant publication-bias concern

### Very Low Certainty

The effect or conclusion is highly uncertain.

Evidence may be:

- Very limited
- Severely biased
- Highly inconsistent
- Indirect
- Extremely imprecise
- Based primarily on case reports, uncontrolled observations, or mechanistic inference

Very-low-certainty evidence must not support confident public claims.

## 8. Factors That Reduce Certainty

Certainty should be reduced when one or more of the following materially affect confidence.

### 8.1 Risk of Bias

Problems in study design, conduct, analysis, or reporting may distort results.

### 8.2 Inconsistency

Important unexplained differences exist across studies.

Assess:

- Direction of effect
- Magnitude of effect
- Confidence-interval overlap
- Statistical heterogeneity
- Clinical heterogeneity
- Methodological heterogeneity

Statistical heterogeneity alone must not be interpreted without clinical context.

### 8.3 Indirectness

Evidence may be indirect because of differences in:

- Population
- Intervention
- Comparator
- Outcome
- Dose
- Formulation
- Duration
- Setting
- Intended use

Evidence from a different ingredient form, dose, or population must not automatically be applied to a NervLock product.

### 8.4 Imprecision

Results may be imprecise because of:

- Small sample size
- Few events
- Wide confidence intervals
- Fragile findings
- Uncertainty across clinically important thresholds

Statistical significance does not eliminate imprecision.

### 8.5 Publication and Dissemination Bias

Positive or favorable findings may be more likely to be:

- Published
- Published rapidly
- Published in English
- Reported fully
- Highlighted publicly

Assess:

- Trial registration
- Unpublished studies
- Small-study effects
- Selective outcome reporting
- Sponsor influence
- Differences between protocol and publication

---

## 9. Factors That May Increase Confidence in Non-Randomized Evidence

In appropriate circumstances, confidence may increase when there is:

- A very large and credible effect
- A consistent dose-response relationship
- Residual confounding likely to reduce rather than create the observed effect
- Strong temporal relationship
- Replication across populations and methods
- Biological coherence supported by human evidence

Upgrading must be conservative.

Biological plausibility alone is not sufficient.

---

## 10. Study-Design Starting Points

For intervention-effect questions:

- Randomized evidence generally begins at higher certainty
- Non-randomized evidence generally begins at lower certainty

Certainty must then be modified according to the total evidence assessment.

This starting rule must not be applied mechanically to:

- Diagnostic questions
- Prognostic questions
- Prevalence questions
- Qualitative questions
- Rare harms
- Long-term safety
- Natural history

Use a design-specific evidence framework for those questions.

## 11. Risk-of-Bias Assessment

Risk of bias must be evaluated by outcome or result when appropriate.

A study must not receive one global quality label without considering which outcome is being interpreted.

### Recommended Methodological Tools

Use the current validated version appropriate to the study design.

Examples include:

- RoB 2 for randomized trials
- ROBINS-I for non-randomized studies of interventions
- ROBINS-E for non-randomized studies of exposures
- QUADAS-3 for diagnostic accuracy studies
- Design-appropriate tools for prognostic, prediction-model, qualitative, and preclinical research

Tools support judgment.

They do not replace methodological expertise.

### Important Distinction

Reporting guidelines and risk-of-bias tools serve different purposes.

Reporting guidelines such as:

- CONSORT
- PRISMA
- STROBE
- STARD
- TRIPOD
- ARRIVE

help assess whether research was reported completely and transparently.

They do not by themselves establish that the study was well designed or unbiased.

A completely reported study may still have high risk of bias.

A poorly reported study may be impossible to judge reliably.

## 12. Randomized Trial Appraisal

When reviewing an RCT, evaluate:

### 12.1 Randomization

- Was the allocation sequence genuinely random?
- Was allocation adequately concealed?
- Could investigators predict the next assignment?

### 12.2 Baseline Balance

- Were clinically important baseline differences present?
- Could differences indicate failure of randomization or chance imbalance?

### 12.3 Blinding

Assess whether lack of blinding could affect:

- Participant behavior
- Co-interventions
- Outcome assessment
- Attrition
- Subjective outcomes

Blinding may be more important for subjective outcomes than for objective outcomes.

### 12.4 Protocol Adherence

- Were deviations balanced?
- Were participants analyzed according to the intended estimand?
- Was intention-to-treat analysis appropriate and correctly performed?

### 12.5 Missing Data

- How much data were missing?
- Why were data missing?
- Were missingness patterns different between groups?
- Could missing data materially change the result?

### 12.6 Outcome Measurement

- Was the outcome valid?
- Was it prespecified?
- Was assessment blinded?
- Was the outcome patient-important or merely a surrogate?

### 12.7 Selective Reporting

Compare:

- Trial registration
- Protocol
- Statistical analysis plan
- Final publication

Look for:

- Omitted outcomes
- Changed primary outcomes
- Changed time points
- Undisclosed subgroup analyses
- Selective reporting of favorable results

### 12.8 Analysis

Evaluate:

- Effect estimate
- Confidence interval
- Absolute effect
- Baseline risk
- Multiplicity
- Subgroup credibility
- Sensitivity analyses
- Clinical importance

---

## 13. Observational Study Appraisal

When reviewing observational evidence, evaluate:

### 13.1 Participant Selection

- Was the sample representative?
- Were inclusion and exclusion criteria appropriate?
- Could selection depend on both exposure and outcome?

### 13.2 Exposure Measurement

- Was exposure measured accurately?
- Was it measured before the outcome?
- Was self-report used?
- Did exposure change over time?
- Was classification differential?

### 13.3 Outcome Measurement

- Was the outcome objectively defined?
- Were assessors blinded to exposure?
- Were diagnostic criteria valid?
- Was follow-up complete?

### 13.4 Confounding

Identify major confounders before interpreting adjusted results.

Consider:

- Age
- Sex
- Socioeconomic status
- Education
- Baseline health
- Medication
- Lifestyle
- Comorbidity
- Healthcare access
- Reverse causation

Statistical adjustment does not guarantee removal of confounding.

### 13.5 Time-Related Bias

Assess:

- Immortal time bias
- Time-varying exposure
- Latency
- Reverse causality
- Inappropriate time origin
- Differential follow-up

### 13.6 Model Integrity

Evaluate:

- Prespecified analysis
- Variable selection
- Overfitting
- Collinearity
- Missing data
- Multiple comparisons
- Sensitivity analyses
- External validation

---

## 14. Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Appraisal

Before using a review, assess:

### 14.1 Question and Protocol

- Was the question clearly defined?
- Was a protocol registered or published?
- Were deviations explained?

### 14.2 Search Strategy

- Were multiple appropriate databases searched?
- Was the search recent enough?
- Were language restrictions justified?
- Was grey literature considered?
- Were trial registries searched?

### 14.3 Study Selection

- Were eligibility criteria prespecified?
- Was screening conducted independently by at least two reviewers?
- Was the exclusion process transparent?

### 14.4 Data Extraction

- Was extraction duplicated?
- Were errors checked?
- Were unpublished or supplemental data sought when necessary?

### 14.5 Risk of Bias

- Was a design-appropriate tool used?
- Was risk of bias incorporated into the conclusion?
- Were high-risk studies separated or tested in sensitivity analyses?

### 14.6 Synthesis

- Were studies sufficiently similar to combine?
- Was the statistical model justified?
- Were zero events handled appropriately?
- Were cluster or crossover designs handled correctly?
- Was heterogeneity explored?

### 14.7 Reporting Bias

- Were missing studies or outcomes considered?
- Were funnel plots interpreted cautiously?
- Were sponsor effects considered?

### 14.8 Certainty

- Was certainty assessed by outcome?
- Did the conclusion reflect certainty?
- Did the abstract overstate the findings?

### 14.9 Currency

A well-conducted but outdated review may not represent current evidence.

Check:

- Search end date
- Major later trials
- Updated guidelines
- Retractions or corrections

## 15. Diagnostic Evidence Appraisal

For diagnostic tests, evaluate:

### 15.1 Intended Use

Clarify whether the test is intended for:

- Screening
- Triage
- Diagnosis
- Rule-in
- Rule-out
- Monitoring
- Prognosis

Accuracy cannot be interpreted without intended use.

### 15.2 Population

- Did the study include the intended clinical population?
- Was a case-control design used with obvious cases and healthy controls?
- Was disease severity representative?
- Was spectrum bias likely?

### 15.3 Index Test

- Was the threshold prespecified?
- Was interpretation blinded to the reference standard?
- Was the test performed consistently?

### 15.4 Reference Standard

- Was the reference standard valid?
- Was it applied equally?
- Could incorporation bias exist?
- Was interpretation blinded?

### 15.5 Flow and Timing

- Did all participants receive verification?
- Was the interval appropriate?
- Could disease status change?
- Were exclusions explained?

### 15.6 Accuracy Measures

Consider:

- Sensitivity
- Specificity
- Likelihood ratios
- Predictive values
- Confidence intervals
- Pretest probability
- Threshold effects

Predictive values depend on prevalence and setting.

High accuracy does not automatically prove clinical utility.

### 15.7 Clinical Utility

Ask whether use of the test improves:

- Patient outcomes
- Decision quality
- Time to diagnosis
- Safety
- Resource use
- Avoidance of unnecessary procedures

Analytical validity, diagnostic accuracy, and clinical utility are separate questions.

---

## 16. Prognostic and Prediction-Model Evidence

Evaluate:

- Clearly defined target population
- Clear outcome definition
- Appropriate follow-up
- Sufficient number of events
- Handling of missing data
- Predictor measurement
- Model development methods
- Overfitting
- Calibration
- Discrimination
- Internal validation
- External validation
- Clinical utility
- Decision thresholds

A model that performs well in development data may fail in new populations.

External validation is essential before broad application.

---

## 17. Mechanistic and Preclinical Evidence

Mechanistic evidence may support plausibility but must be classified separately from clinical outcome evidence.

### In Vitro Evidence

May identify:

- Molecular pathways
- Receptor effects
- Cellular responses
- Toxicity signals

Limitations include:

- Non-physiologic concentrations
- Simplified systems
- Lack of metabolism
- Lack of whole-organism interaction
- Uncertain human relevance

### Animal Evidence

May support:

- Biological plausibility
- Dose exploration
- Safety signals
- Mechanistic hypotheses

Limitations include:

- Species differences
- Model validity
- Dose translation
- Publication bias
- Limited replication
- Outcome mismatch

### Human Mechanistic Evidence

Examples:

- Imaging
- Biomarkers
- Neurophysiology
- Pharmacokinetics
- Experimental challenge studies

Human mechanistic evidence remains distinct from proof of patient-important benefit.

### Communication Rule

Do not convert:

> “This pathway was affected”

into:

> “This improves brain health”

without direct clinical evidence.

---

## 18. Surrogate Outcomes and Biomarkers

A surrogate outcome is not automatically equivalent to clinical benefit.

Examples may include:

- Blood biomarkers
- Inflammatory markers
- Imaging findings
- Neurotrophic factors
- Oxidative stress markers
- Short cognitive test scores
- EEG measures
- Microbiome composition

Before relying on a surrogate, ask:

- Is the surrogate validated?
- Does changing it reliably improve meaningful outcomes?
- Is the relationship causal?
- Is the magnitude clinically important?
- Could the intervention affect the surrogate without affecting health?

Biomarker improvement must not be described as disease prevention or functional improvement unless supported by appropriate evidence.

---

## 19. Clinical Importance

Statistical significance is not the same as clinical importance.

Every effect should be evaluated for:

- Magnitude
- Absolute difference
- Confidence interval
- Baseline risk
- Minimal clinically important difference
- Duration
- Patient relevance
- Burden
- Harms
- Cost
- Feasibility

A very small statistically significant change may have little practical value.

A non-significant result may still be compatible with clinically important benefit or harm if the confidence interval is wide.

---

## 20. Relative and Absolute Effects

When possible, report both:

- Relative effect
- Absolute effect

Examples include:

- Risk ratio
- Odds ratio
- Hazard ratio
- Absolute risk difference
- Number needed to treat
- Number needed to harm

Relative effects can appear large when absolute risk is small.

Absolute effects depend on baseline risk.

Do not present relative risk reduction alone when it may mislead readers.

---

## 21. Confidence Intervals

Confidence intervals should be used to communicate:

- Precision
- Compatible effect sizes
- Uncertainty
- Whether clinically meaningful benefit or harm remains possible

Do not interpret a confidence interval only according to whether it crosses a null value.

Consider whether it crosses clinically important thresholds.

---

## 22. Subgroup Analyses

Subgroup findings require caution.

Stronger credibility requires:

- Prespecification
- Limited number of analyses
- Biological or clinical rationale
- Formal interaction testing
- Adequate sample size
- Consistency across studies
- Replication

A significant result in one subgroup and a non-significant result in another does not prove a true subgroup difference.

Exploratory subgroup findings must be labeled exploratory.

---

## 23. Dose-Response Evidence

Dose-response relationships may strengthen causal interpretation, but evaluate:

- Whether dose was measured accurately
- Whether the relationship is linear
- Whether reverse causality is possible
- Whether higher dose increases harms
- Whether the studied dose matches the proposed product
- Whether formulation affects absorption
- Whether effects plateau
- Whether threshold effects exist

More is not automatically better.

---

## 24. Safety and Harm Assessment

Benefit evidence must never be reviewed without safety evidence.

Assess:

- Common adverse effects
- Serious adverse effects
- Discontinuation
- Mortality where relevant
- Drug interactions
- Disease interactions
- Laboratory abnormalities
- Dose-related toxicity
- Pregnancy and lactation
- Pediatric use
- Older adults
- Hepatic impairment
- Renal impairment
- Bleeding risk
- Perioperative risk
- Long-term exposure
- Contamination and product quality

Absence of reported harm is not proof of safety.

Studies often measure benefits more thoroughly than harms.

---

## 25. Nutrition and Supplement Evidence

Supplement evidence requires additional scrutiny.

### 25.1 Ingredient Identity

Confirm:

- Chemical form
- Botanical species
- Plant part
- Extract
- Standardization
- Salt form
- Isomer
- Purity
- Manufacturing quality

Evidence for one form must not automatically be applied to another.

### 25.2 Dose

Compare:

- Studied dose
- Proposed dose
- Daily intake
- Duration
- Frequency
- Loading dose
- Background dietary intake

A product containing an ingredient does not automatically reproduce the study result.

### 25.3 Population

Consider whether benefit depends on:

- Deficiency
- Baseline nutrient status
- Age
- Disease state
- Genetics
- Diet
- Medication
- Geographic population

Benefits in deficient populations may not apply to nutrient-replete consumers.

### 25.4 Formulation

Evaluate:

- Bioavailability
- Stability
- Co-ingredients
- Delivery system
- Food effects
- Pharmacokinetics
- Combination interactions

Evidence for an isolated ingredient may not validate a multi-ingredient formulation.

### 25.5 Outcomes

Distinguish:

- Laboratory changes
- Symptom scales
- Cognitive test scores
- Functional outcomes
- Disease outcomes
- Quality of life
- Adverse effects

### 25.6 Duration

Short studies may not establish:

- Sustained benefit
- Long-term safety
- Disease prevention
- Adherence
- Real-world effectiveness

### 25.7 Industry Funding

Industry funding does not automatically invalidate research.

However, assess:

- Sponsor role
- Data ownership
- Analysis control
- Publication rights
- Selective reporting
- Author conflicts
- Independent replication

### 25.8 Product-Level Evidence

Ingredient-level evidence and product-level evidence are different.

A finished product claim requires consideration of:

- Exact ingredients
- Exact doses
- Exact forms
- Manufacturing
- Stability
- Interactions
- Target population
- Finished-product testing

Do not imply that ingredient evidence automatically proves the efficacy of the finished product.

---

## 26. Functional Medicine Evidence

Functional medicine concepts must be evaluated using the same evidence standards as all other medical concepts.

Do not lower the evidence threshold because a concept is:

- Holistic
- Personalized
- Systems-based
- Mechanistically plausible
- Popular
- Clinically fashionable

Evaluate separately:

- Validity of the proposed mechanism
- Accuracy of diagnostic testing
- Reproducibility
- Clinical utility
- Intervention effectiveness
- Safety
- Cost
- Opportunity cost

Terms such as:

- Root cause
- Detoxification
- Leaky gut
- Adrenal fatigue
- Neuroinflammation
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Hormonal imbalance

must not be used as established diagnoses or causal explanations without precise definition and adequate support.

A systems-oriented perspective is permitted.

Unsupported certainty is not.

---

## 27. Guideline Appraisal

When using a clinical guideline, record:

- Full title
- Issuing organization
- Publication year
- Version
- Target population
- Jurisdiction
- Recommendation
- Strength of recommendation
- Certainty of evidence
- Update date
- Conflict-of-interest process

Determine whether the guideline:

- Used a systematic evidence review
- Used a transparent grading method
- Included relevant stakeholders
- Managed conflicts appropriately
- Considered benefits and harms
- Considered patient values
- Applies to the intended setting

When rigorous guidelines disagree:

1. Identify the disagreement
2. Compare evidence dates
3. Compare populations
4. Compare methods
5. Compare thresholds and values
6. Present the disagreement transparently
7. Avoid declaring one position correct without sufficient basis

## 28. Evidence-to-Decision Separation

Evidence certainty and recommendation strength are not identical.

A recommendation may depend on:

- Benefits
- Harms
- Certainty
- Patient values
- Feasibility
- Acceptability
- Cost
- Equity
- Resource use
- Alternatives

High-certainty evidence of a small effect does not automatically justify a strong recommendation.

Low-certainty evidence may occasionally support urgent action when:

- Risk is severe
- Alternatives are limited
- Potential benefit is substantial
- Harms are low
- Waiting for better evidence is unreasonable

Such judgments must be explicit.

---

## 29. Applicability and Generalizability

Before applying evidence, compare the study with the intended audience.

Evaluate:

- Age
- Sex
- Ethnicity
- Geography
- Baseline risk
- Disease severity
- Comorbidity
- Medication
- Nutritional status
- Healthcare setting
- Adherence
- Dose
- Duration
- Product formulation

Do not assume that evidence from:

- Highly selected trial populations
- Another country
- A different age group
- A deficient population
- A specific disease group
- A different product formulation

fully applies to the general NervLock audience.

---

## 30. Conflicts of Interest

For important evidence, review:

- Funding source
- Sponsor role
- Author disclosures
- Patents
- Advisory relationships
- Employment
- Data access
- Statistical analysis control
- Publication control
- Ghostwriting risk

Conflict of interest does not automatically invalidate evidence.

It increases the need for:

- Methodological scrutiny
- Independent replication
- Transparent disclosure
- Conservative interpretation

---

## 31. Retractions, Corrections, and Research Integrity

Before relying on high-impact evidence, check when feasible for:

- Retraction
- Expression of concern
- Major correction
- Data integrity concern
- Duplicate publication
- Implausible results
- Unresolved methodological criticism

Retracted evidence must not be used as valid support.

Corrected studies must be interpreted using the corrected record.

---

## 32. Evidence Recency

Recency alone does not determine quality.

However, current verification is required when evidence may have changed.

Check for:

- Updated guidelines
- New pivotal trials
- Updated systematic reviews
- Safety warnings
- Regulatory decisions
- Retractions
- Major contradictory evidence

Record:

- Search date
- Evidence cutoff date
- Guideline version
- Last review date

Evergreen content must still be updated when material evidence changes.

---

## 33. Contradictory Evidence

When evidence conflicts:

1. Do not select only the favorable studies
2. Identify the conflicting findings
3. Compare study quality
4. Compare populations
5. Compare interventions and doses
6. Compare outcomes and follow-up
7. Evaluate risk of bias
8. Evaluate chance and imprecision
9. Evaluate sponsor effects
10. State the remaining uncertainty

Preferred language:

> “Studies have produced inconsistent findings, and the reason for the difference is not yet clear.”

Do not create false consensus.

Do not create false balance when one side is supported by substantially stronger evidence.

---

## 34. Absence of Evidence

Distinguish:

### Evidence of No Meaningful Effect

Adequate evidence excludes a clinically important effect with reasonable confidence.

### No Evidence of Effect

Available research has not demonstrated an effect.

### Insufficient Evidence

The evidence is too limited, biased, indirect, or imprecise to reach a reliable conclusion.

### No Relevant Studies Found

No eligible evidence was identified using the stated search approach.

Do not interpret “not proven” as “proven ineffective.”

Do not interpret “not shown to be harmful” as “proven safe.”

---

## 35. NervLock Public Communication Categories

NervLock converts evidence certainty into public language using the following categories.

### Category A — Established Evidence

Criteria may include:

- High or moderate certainty
- Consistent clinical evidence
- Relevant outcomes
- Appropriate guideline support
- No major unresolved contradiction

Preferred language:

- “현재 근거는 비교적 일관됩니다.”
- “여러 임상연구와 지침에서 지지됩니다.”
- “현재 임상적으로 인정되는 접근입니다.”

Avoid absolute language such as:

- Always
- Guaranteed
- Completely proven
- Works for everyone

### Category B — Supported but Qualified

Criteria may include:

- Moderate or limited certainty
- Reasonably consistent findings
- Relevant limitations
- Incomplete long-term evidence

Preferred language:

- “도움이 될 가능성이 있습니다.”
- “일부 임상연구에서 긍정적인 결과가 보고되었습니다.”
- “다만 효과의 크기와 적용 대상에는 불확실성이 남아 있습니다.”

### Category C — Emerging Evidence

Criteria may include:

- Early human studies
- Small trials
- Inconsistent findings
- Limited replication
- Surrogate outcomes

Preferred language:

- “초기 연구에서 가능성이 제시되었습니다.”
- “현재 근거는 제한적입니다.”
- “확정적인 결론을 내리기에는 추가 연구가 필요합니다.”

### Category D — Mechanistic or Hypothesis-Level Evidence

Criteria may include:

- Laboratory evidence
- Animal evidence
- Biological rationale
- Human biomarker evidence without clinical outcomes

Preferred language:

- “이론적으로 제안된 기전입니다.”
- “실험실 또는 동물연구에서 관찰되었으나 사람에서의 임상적 효과는 확인되지 않았습니다.”
- “생물학적 가능성과 실제 임상효과는 구분해야 합니다.”

### Category E — Unsupported, Contradicted, or Misleading

Criteria may include:

- No credible evidence
- Evidence of no meaningful benefit
- Serious methodological flaws
- Misrepresentation of mechanism
- Disproportionate claim
- Contradiction by stronger evidence

Required action:

- Do not present as a benefit
- Correct misleading statements
- Explain the limitation
- Do not use for product promotion

## 36. Evidence Language Matrix

### High Certainty

Use:

- Demonstrates
- Reduces
- Increases
- Is associated with, when the evidence is observational
- Is recommended, when citing an applicable guideline

Still specify:

- Population
- Outcome
- Magnitude
- Conditions
- Limitations where relevant

### Moderate Certainty

Use:

- Probably
- Likely
- Evidence suggests
- Is reasonably supported

### Low Certainty

Use:

- May
- Might
- Limited evidence suggests
- Some studies report

### Very Low Certainty

Use:

- It is uncertain whether
- Evidence is insufficient
- A hypothesis has been proposed
- Clinical benefit has not been established

### Prohibited Inflation

Do not convert:

- “May support” → “Improves”
- “Associated with” → “Prevents”
- “Biologically plausible” → “Proven”
- “Changes a biomarker” → “Protects the brain”
- “One small trial” → “Clinical evidence confirms”
- “Ingredient evidence” → “Finished product is effective”

## 37. Product Claim Evidence Threshold

Before proposing a product-related statement, determine:

### Level 1 — Ingredient Description

Example:

- Source
- Chemical form
- Physiological role
- General nutritional context

Requires:

- Accurate factual support
- No implied clinical outcome beyond evidence and regulatory permission

### Level 2 — Mechanistic Rationale

Example:

- Participation in a biological pathway

Requires:

- Mechanistic evidence
- Clear statement that mechanism does not prove clinical benefit

### Level 3 — Functional Support

Example:

- Support of normal physiological function

Requires:

- Relevant human evidence
- Dose relevance
- Formulation relevance
- Regulatory review

### Level 4 — Symptom or Performance Benefit

Requires:

- Direct human clinical evidence
- Relevant outcome
- Relevant population
- Adequate dose and duration
- Acceptable safety
- Regulatory review

### Level 5 — Disease Risk, Prevention, or Treatment

Requires:

- Strong direct clinical evidence
- Applicable legal authorization
- Medical and regulatory review
- Final human approval

For supplements and general consumer products, this level will frequently be prohibited or inappropriate even when scientific literature exists.

Scientific plausibility does not create regulatory permission.

## 40. AI Evidence Review Rules

AI may assist with:

- Literature discovery
- PICO formulation
- Study classification
- Evidence tables
- Risk-of-bias prompts
- Data extraction
- Comparison of findings
- Draft summaries
- Identifying limitations
- Language calibration

AI must not:

- Invent references
- Infer study methods from titles alone
- Claim full-text review when only an abstract was accessed
- Assign certainty without explaining the basis
- Treat citation count as evidence quality
- Treat journal prestige as evidence quality
- Resolve conflicting evidence by popularity
- Hide unfavorable evidence
- Generate a product claim before evidence review
- Approve its own high-risk conclusion

AI-generated appraisal must remain reviewable.

Important judgments must identify the evidence and rationale supporting them.

## 41. Evidence Search Requirements

For formal reviews, use a structured search appropriate to the risk and purpose.

Prioritize:

1. Current clinical guidelines
2. Systematic reviews
3. Pivotal randomized trials
4. Major observational studies
5. Safety databases and official warnings
6. Primary mechanistic studies when clinically relevant

For high-risk product or disease claims:

- Search more than one reliable database or official source where feasible
- Review recent evidence
- Check major contradictory findings
- Check retraction or correction status
- Verify primary sources
- Record the search date

A casual internet search is not sufficient for a high-risk evidence review.

---

## 42. Reporting Guideline Use

When evaluating completeness of reporting, consider the current design-specific guideline.

Examples include:

- CONSORT for randomized trials
- PRISMA for systematic reviews
- STROBE for observational studies
- STARD for diagnostic accuracy studies
- TRIPOD for prediction models
- ARRIVE for animal studies

Do not use reporting-checklist completion as a numerical quality score.

Reporting completeness informs confidence in appraisal.

It does not replace risk-of-bias assessment.

## 43. Evidence Review Status Labels

### Usable

The evidence and proposed interpretation are sufficiently supported for the stated internal purpose.

Public use may still require regulatory review.

### Usable With Qualification

The evidence supports cautious use with explicit limitations.

### Revision Recommended

The interpretation overstates, omits, or misclassifies evidence.

### Insufficient Evidence

Available evidence cannot support the proposed conclusion.

### Not Recommended

The claim is unsupported, contradicted, misleading, or unsafe.

### Specialist Review Required

The topic requires additional domain expertise.

### Regulatory Review Required

The scientific interpretation may be valid, but public or commercial use requires compliance review.

## 44. Pre-Publication Scientific Checklist

Before scientific or medical content is approved, confirm:

1. Is the question clearly defined?
2. Is the study design appropriate to the question?
3. Were the most relevant current sources reviewed?
4. Are primary sources verified?
5. Is risk of bias addressed?
6. Is inconsistency addressed?
7. Is indirectness addressed?
8. Is imprecision addressed?
9. Is publication bias considered?
10. Are effect size and clinical importance separated?
11. Are absolute effects reported where useful?
12. Are patient-important outcomes prioritized?
13. Are surrogate outcomes identified?
14. Are harms and interactions addressed?
15. Is the population applicable?
16. Does the studied dose match the discussed dose?
17. Does the studied formulation match the discussed formulation?
18. Is mechanism separated from clinical outcome?
19. Is association separated from causation?
20. Is statistical significance separated from clinical significance?
21. Are conflicts of interest disclosed?
22. Are contradictions acknowledged?
23. Is uncertainty stated proportionately?
24. Is the public language no stronger than the evidence?
25. Does the content require regulatory review?
26. Has final human review occurred?

If a material item remains unresolved, the content is not ready for publication.

## 45. Governance

This standard governs scientific evaluation.

It does not independently determine:

- Legal permissibility
- Advertising compliance
- Product authorization
- Clinical diagnosis
- Individualized treatment
- Final publication approval

When scientific evidence and commercial objectives conflict, scientific integrity takes priority.

When scientific evidence and regulatory permission differ, both must be satisfied before a public commercial claim is used.

Material evidence judgments require review by an appropriately qualified human reviewer.

## 46. Final Medical Evidence Principles

Evidence is not a collection of favorable citations.

Evidence is the critical evaluation of the complete and relevant body of research.

A mechanism is not an outcome.

An association is not causation.

Statistical significance is not clinical importance.

A meta-analysis is not automatically high quality.

A guideline is not automatically authoritative.

A physician’s opinion is not automatically evidence.

An ingredient study is not automatically a finished-product study.

Absence of reported harm is not proof of safety.

Uncertainty must not be hidden.

When the evidence is strong, communicate clearly.

When the evidence is limited, communicate cautiously.

When the evidence is insufficient, say so.

When a claim exceeds the evidence, do not publish it.

NervLock’s scientific credibility depends not on sounding certain, but on being appropriately certain.
