## 1. Purpose

This document defines the regulatory and advertising compliance rules governing NervLock.

It is designed to reduce the risk that NervLock content, products, services, endorsements, or commercial activities may:

- Mislead consumers
- Imply unauthorized disease prevention or treatment
- Cause a food or supplement to be mistaken for a medicine
- Cause an ordinary food to be mistaken for a health functional food
- Overstate scientific evidence
- Misuse physician authority
- Violate medical advertising restrictions
- Conceal commercial relationships
- Misrepresent testimonials or before-and-after results
- Omit material safety information
- Use unverified superiority or comparative claims
- Infringe privacy or patient confidentiality
- Create inconsistent claims across channels
- Publish content before required review or self-review

This document is an internal compliance framework.

It does not replace:

- Current statutes and regulations
- Official regulatory guidance
- Product-specific review
- Medical advertising review
- Food or health functional food self-review
- Qualified legal advice
- Final human approval

When uncertainty remains, choose the more conservative wording and require specialist review before publication.

---

## 2. Governing Principle

NervLock follows one central regulatory rule:

> Scientific evidence does not automatically create legal permission to advertise a claim.

A statement may be:

- Scientifically plausible but legally prohibited
- Accurate in a journal but misleading in consumer advertising
- Appropriate in disease education but inappropriate beside a product
- Permissible for one product category but prohibited for another
- Acceptable internally but unsuitable for public-facing use
- Permissible in one country but not in Korea

Every claim must satisfy both:

1. Scientific support
2. Regulatory permission for the exact product, service, audience, channel, and context

If either requirement is not satisfied, the claim must not be published.

## 6. Commercial Nexus Test

Educational content may acquire a promotional character when it is connected to a commercial objective.

The following factors increase regulatory risk:

- Product name or image
- Product logo
- Purchase link
- Price
- Discount
- Coupon
- “Shop now” or similar CTA
- Repeated product placement
- Affiliate link
- Sponsorship
- Physician endorsement
- Customer testimonial
- Product-specific mechanism
- Product-specific ingredient discussion
- Product availability announcement
- Retargeting or paid distribution
- Disease content followed by a product recommendation

No single factor is always decisive.

Evaluate the overall impression from the perspective of an ordinary consumer.

A disclaimer such as “educational content only” does not neutralize a strongly promotional overall impression.

## 7. Overall-Impression Rule

Compliance is determined by the total impression created by:

- Words
- Headlines
- Images
- Audio
- Music
- Editing
- Visual sequence
- Icons
- Graphs
- Footnotes
- Disclaimers
- Product placement
- Links
- Hashtags
- Metadata
- Structured data
- Comments pinned by the account
- Creator or physician identity

A technically accurate sentence may still be misleading when combined with a dramatic image, product placement, or purchase CTA.

The main claim cannot be corrected by:

- Small text
- Fast speech
- Hidden captions
- Hashtags
- A footer disclaimer
- A link to detailed limitations
- A general statement that results vary

Material qualifications must be clear, proximate, readable, audible, and understandable.

## 8. NervLock Regulatory Risk Scale

### Green — Generally Usable

Characteristics:

- Pure education
- No product or service promotion
- Accurate and balanced
- No disease-treatment implication
- No unsupported comparative claim
- No identifiable patient information
- No hidden commercial relationship

Action:

- Factual and brand review
- Reference verification where medical claims are included

### Yellow — Use With Qualification

Characteristics:

- Product-adjacent education
- Qualified functional language
- Emerging evidence
- Disease names used in general education
- Physician identity present without direct endorsement
- Comparison based on objective data
- Limited commercial connection

Action:

- Scientific review
- Regulatory review
- Clear limitations
- Final human approval before publication where material

### Orange — High Risk; Revision Required

Characteristics:

- Symptom-improvement claim
- Physician recommendation
- Product linked to disease content
- Before-and-after content
- Testimonials describing efficacy
- Unverified superiority claim
- “Clinically proven” or similar language
- Strong mechanism-to-benefit inference
- Required self-review or pre-review not confirmed

Action:

- Do not publish as drafted
- Rewrite conservatively
- Obtain specialist review
- Confirm review or self-review obligations

### Red — Prohibited or Presumptively Unpublishable

Characteristics:

- Disease prevention or treatment claim for food or supplement
- Medicine-like claim
- General food presented as health functional food
- Fabricated or manipulated testimonial
- Guaranteed outcome
- Instruction to replace or stop medical treatment
- False physician endorsement
- Unreviewed medical advertisement where review is required
- Hidden paid relationship
- Materially deceptive before-and-after content
- False or unverifiable “No. 1,” “best,” or superiority claim

Action:

- Do not publish
- Record the reason
- Provide a compliant alternative if possible
- Escalate when strategically necessary

## 9. Claim Ladder

Every proposed claim must be assigned to the highest applicable level.

### Level 0 — Neutral Identification

Examples:

- Product name
- Ingredient name
- Content amount
- Manufacturer
- General brand identity

Risk:

- Low if accurate and not misleading

### Level 1 — General Educational Description

Examples:

- Normal physiology
- Nutrient role
- General lifestyle education
- Definition of a condition

Risk:

- Low to moderate
- Must remain separate from implied product outcomes

### Level 2 — Mechanistic Statement

Examples:

- Participation in a pathway
- Receptor interaction
- Biomarker relationship

Risk:

- Moderate
- Mechanism does not prove clinical benefit

### Level 3 — Normal Functional Support

Examples:

- Supports a normal physiological function
- Helps maintain an authorized function

Risk:

- Moderate to high
- Requires category-specific legal basis, exact conditions, and review

### Level 4 — Symptom or Performance Benefit

Examples:

- Improves concentration
- Reduces fatigue
- Helps memory
- Improves sleep quality

Risk:

- High
- Requires direct evidence and category-specific legal permission

### Level 5 — Disease Risk Reduction or Prevention

Examples:

- Prevents dementia
- Lowers Parkinson’s disease risk
- Prevents neuropathy

Risk:

- Very high
- Commonly prohibited for foods and supplements unless a specific lawful framework applies

### Level 6 — Diagnosis, Treatment, Cure, or Replacement

Examples:

- Treats Alzheimer’s disease
- Reverses brain aging
- Cures brain fog
- Replaces medication
- Works like a prescription drug

Risk:

- Red
- Do not use for food or supplement promotion

AI must not move a claim upward on this ladder without explicit scientific and regulatory approval.

## 10. Food Advertising Core Rules

Food-related content must not create a reasonable consumer impression that the food:

- Prevents or treats disease
- Is a medicine
- Has effects beyond its legally permitted category
- Is a health functional food when it is not
- Has guaranteed or exaggerated efficacy
- Has qualities not supported by evidence
- Is superior without objective substantiation
- Has official approval, certification, or endorsement that it does not have

Food advertising must be evaluated for:

- Product category
- Exact wording
- Implied meaning
- Images and symbols
- Disease terminology
- Expert statements
- Consumer testimonials
- Evidence
- Required self-review
- Approved function
- Target population
- Warnings and precautions

---

## 11. General Food Rules

A general food must not be presented as:

- A medicine
- A treatment
- A disease-prevention product
- A health functional food
- A clinically proven therapeutic intervention

General foods must not use a health functional food logo or imitate the appearance of an authorized health functional food in a misleading way.

Functional or health-related wording may be used only when:

- A current legal basis permits it
- All required conditions are satisfied
- The wording stays within the authorized scope
- Required evidence is held
- Required self-review has been completed
- Mandatory notices and labeling are applied

The existence of a study on an ingredient does not independently authorize a general-food claim.

---

## 12. Health Functional Food Rules

Health functional food content must be based on:

- The legally recognized product category
- Authorized functional ingredients
- Authorized functions
- Approved conditions of use
- Actual ingredient form
- Actual dose
- Actual finished formulation
- Applicable warnings
- Required labeling and self-review

### NervLock Must

- Confirm the exact authorized function
- Confirm the exact ingredient and form
- Confirm the daily intake
- Confirm target and excluded populations
- Confirm warnings and interactions
- Use self-reviewed wording where required
- Preserve the meaning of approved wording across all channels
- Maintain the approval and evidence record

### NervLock Must Not

- Add disease names to increase persuasive effect
- Convert an authorized function into a treatment claim
- Convert “may help” into “improves” without authorization
- Use a stronger claim in a headline than in approved body copy
- Treat ingredient-level evidence as finished-product proof
- Omit material limitations
- Alter reviewed advertising beyond permitted changes
- Imply that health functional food approval equals medicine approval

Any variation for short-form video, thumbnails, hashtags, captions, or influencer scripts must be reviewed separately.

---

## 13. Functional Claims for Ordinary Foods

When an ordinary food is intended to use functional wording:

1. Confirm that the applicable functional-labeling framework permits it
2. Confirm ingredient eligibility
3. Confirm content and manufacturing requirements
4. Confirm evidence requirements
5. Confirm wording conditions
6. Complete required self-review
7. Apply mandatory notices
8. Archive the evidence and review result
9. Re-review every derivative asset

Do not use a disclaimer to compensate for failure to meet the legal framework.

If eligibility is uncertain, treat the claim as prohibited until confirmed.

## 14. Disease Language

Disease names may be used in genuine medical education.

Risk increases sharply when disease language appears with:

- A product
- An ingredient
- A purchase link
- A discount
- A physician recommendation
- A testimonial
- A branded formulation
- A “solution” narrative
- A visual showing disease reversal
- A call to action

### General Educational Use

Potentially acceptable:

- Definition
- Symptoms
- Risk factors
- Diagnostic process
- Treatment overview
- When to seek care
- Guideline discussion

### Product-Adjacent Use

Requires heightened review.

Avoid structures such as:

> Disease fear → biological mechanism → product ingredient → purchase CTA

Disease-related education should be separated from product promotion by:

- Page purpose
- Visual hierarchy
- links
- wording
- CTA
- review process

A disease disclaimer does not cure a product-centered disease message.

## 15. Symptom Claims

Symptoms such as the following may function as medical or disease-related claims depending on context:

- Memory loss
- Cognitive decline
- Brain fog
- Fatigue
- Poor concentration
- Insomnia
- Tremor
- Neuropathic pain
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Anxiety
- Depression

Before using symptom language:

- Define whether the content is educational or promotional
- Determine whether the symptom may indicate disease
- Avoid guaranteeing symptom improvement
- Avoid encouraging self-diagnosis
- Include medical-evaluation guidance when appropriate
- Confirm that product wording is legally permitted

“Brain fog” is not a regulatory safe harbor.

Using a nontechnical symptom term can still create a treatment implication.

## 16. Medical Advertising Rules

Content may be medical advertising when it communicates information about:

- A medical institution
- A physician
- A medical service
- A medical procedure
- Diagnosis
- Treatment
- Clinical outcomes
- Medical pricing
- Appointment booking

NervLock must determine whether the Medical Service Act applies before publication.

### Prohibited or High-Risk Medical Advertising Includes

- Advertising by an unauthorized person
- Advertising unassessed new medical technology
- Treatment-experience testimonials likely to mislead about efficacy
- False content
- Comparative claims concerning another medical provider or treatment method
- Defamation of another medical provider
- Direct exposure of surgical or procedural scenes
- Omission of serious adverse effects or other material information
- Exaggeration of objective facts
- Use of unauthorized qualifications or titles
- Advertorial or expert-opinion formats that conceal advertising
- Advertising not reviewed, or materially different from reviewed content, where review is required
- Misleading discounts or waivers of non-covered medical fees
- Unauthorized award, certification, guarantee, or recommendation claims
- Other methods likely to harm public health or fair medical competition

### Medical Advertising Review

Where medical advertising review is legally required:

- Obtain review before publication
- Publish only the reviewed version
- Review thumbnails, captions, subtitles, landing pages, and derivative edits
- Archive the review number, reviewed asset, date, and approval scope
- Re-submit material changes

A reviewed advertisement may still require re-review if its meaning or presentation changes.

---

## 17. Education Versus Medical Advertising

Physician-authored medical education is not automatically medical advertising.

However, educational content may be treated as promotional when it:

- Identifies a specific clinic or physician repeatedly
- Promotes appointment booking
- Highlights proprietary treatment
- Includes prices or discounts
- Uses patient outcomes
- Uses superiority claims
- Contains strong conversion CTAs
- Is distributed as paid promotion
- Leads directly to booking or sales
- Uses fear to prompt consultation

The content must be evaluated as a whole.

NervLock should maintain clear separation among:

- General brain health education
- Physician biography
- Medical service information
- Clinic promotion
- Product promotion

## 18. Physician Identity and Authority

Physician credentials may establish authorship and professional context.

They must not be used to create an unsupported guarantee.

### Generally Safer

- Accurate name
- Verified license status
- Lawfully recognized specialty
- Accurate training and career history
- Clear authorship
- Evidence-based explanation

### High Risk

- “A neurologist recommends this for everyone”
- “Doctor-developed, so it definitely works”
- “Hospital-grade supplement”
- “The formula doctors trust”
- “Clinically guaranteed by a specialist”
- White-coat imagery combined with unsupported efficacy claims

The physician’s title cannot substitute for:

- Evidence
- Product authorization
- Advertising review
- Safety evaluation
- Regulatory permission

Only legally recognized qualifications and specialties may be used.

---

## 19. Separation of Medical Education and Product Commerce

Because NervLock is neurologist-led and may sell products, it must apply heightened separation controls.

### Required Separation Controls

- Clearly identify whether a page is educational or commercial
- Do not disguise product advertising as neutral medical education
- Do not place purchase CTAs inside urgent disease warnings
- Do not use clinical authority to pressure purchase
- Do not use patient consultations as sales funnels without clear boundaries
- Disclose commercial relationships
- Use separate review workflows for disease education and product promotion
- Avoid automatic product recommendation at the end of every disease article

The reader should be able to understand:

- Who is speaking
- Whether a product is being sold
- Whether the statement is education or advertising
- What evidence supports the statement
- What medical limitations apply

---

## 20. Testimonials and Consumer Reviews

Testimonials are not proof of efficacy.

They must not be used to communicate a claim that NervLock could not lawfully make directly.

### Required Conditions

- The testimonial must be genuine
- The speaker’s experience must not be fabricated
- Material editing must not change meaning
- Compensation or incentives must be disclosed
- Exceptional results must not be presented as typical
- Product, dose, duration, and relevant context should not be concealed when material
- Consent and usage rights must be documented
- Health information must be handled lawfully

### Prohibited or High-Risk Uses

- Invented reviews
- AI-generated patient stories presented as real
- Edited reviews that intensify efficacy
- Review selection designed to create a false typical result
- Disease-treatment testimonials for food or supplements
- Medical treatment-experience testimonials likely to mislead
- Testimonials used to bypass claim restrictions

“Individual experience” disclaimers do not automatically make an unlawful or misleading testimonial acceptable.

---

## 21. Before-and-After Content

Before-and-after content is presumptively high risk.

Before use, confirm:

- Whether the content is medical advertising
- Whether the product category permits the implication
- Whether the result is typical
- Whether conditions are comparable
- Whether time period is stated
- Whether other interventions are disclosed
- Whether images are retouched
- Whether lighting, posture, makeup, angle, or editing creates deception
- Whether valid consent exists
- Whether a required review has been completed

Never use before-and-after content to imply:

- Guaranteed response
- Disease reversal
- Structural brain repair
- Universal effectiveness
- A result caused solely by the product when causality is uncertain

For medical treatment promotion, use only after specialist review and any required medical-advertising review.

## 22. Influencers, Affiliates, Ambassadors, and Sponsored Content

Material commercial relationships must be disclosed clearly.

Relationships include:

- Payment
- Free products
- Discounts
- Affiliate commissions
- Travel
- Services
- Gifts
- Employment
- Equity
- Family or business relationships
- Performance bonuses

Disclosure must be:

- Clear
- Prominent
- In the same language as the content
- Easy to notice before or with the recommendation
- Repeated when needed in video or multi-part content
- Not hidden among hashtags
- Not placed only after “more”
- Not replaced by vague wording

The brand remains responsible for claims made by partners on its behalf.

Provide approved scripts and prohibited-claim rules.

Monitor published content and require correction or removal when necessary.

## 23. Expert Endorsements

An expert may explain evidence within the expert’s actual field.

An expert endorsement must not:

- Exceed the person’s qualifications
- Imply personal use when none occurred
- Guarantee results
- Substitute opinion for evidence
- Misstate the product category
- Convert a food into a medicine-like product
- Conceal compensation
- Use an unauthorized title
- State that a regulatory authority recommends the product without proof

NervLock must maintain:

- Credential verification
- Conflict-of-interest disclosure
- Script approval
- Claim substantiation
- Consent and contract
- Publication record

## 24. Comparative, Superiority, and “No. 1” Claims

Comparative claims require objective and current substantiation.

Before use, define:

- Compared products or category
- Comparison period
- Geography
- Metric
- Data source
- Testing conditions
- Sample
- Statistical method
- Material limitations

Claims such as the following are high risk:

- Best
- No. 1
- Most trusted
- Leading
- Superior
- Strongest
- Highest absorption
- More effective
- Clinically better
- Doctor preferred

A narrow result must not be broadened.

Example:

> Highest measured amount among three products tested under one laboratory condition

must not become:

> Korea’s best brain supplement

Internal aspirations such as “Korea’s most trusted” must not be used publicly without objective and current support.

## 25. Certifications, Awards, Approvals, and Institutional Names

Do not imply endorsement by:

- MFDS
- Ministry of Health and Welfare
- FDA
- EFSA
- A university
- A hospital
- A medical society
- A research institute
- A public authority

unless the exact relationship and permitted wording are verified.

### Required Verification

- What was certified or approved
- Who issued it
- Validity period
- Scope
- Conditions
- Whether public use is permitted
- Whether the mark or logo may be used
- Whether the claim concerns safety, quality, function, manufacturing, or efficacy

Do not convert:

- Facility certification into product efficacy
- Ingredient recognition into finished-product efficacy
- Regulatory registration into medical endorsement
- Foreign permission into Korean permission
- Publication in a journal into clinical approval

Foreign regulatory status must be described precisely and must not be presented as authorization for use in Korea.

## 26. “Clinically Proven,” “Science-Backed,” and Similar Claims

These phrases are high risk because they create a strong overall impression.

### “Clinically Proven”

Do not use unless all of the following are verified:

- Exact product or exact formulation was studied
- Relevant human clinical study
- Appropriate population
- Appropriate comparator
- Appropriate dose and duration
- Clinically relevant outcome
- Adequate study quality
- Results support the exact claim
- No material contradictory evidence is concealed
- Regulatory use is permitted
- Final human approval is documented

### “Science-Backed”

Avoid as a broad marketing label.

Preferred alternatives:

- “근거를 검토해 설계한”
- “공개된 연구와 안전성 자료를 바탕으로 검토한”
- “과학적 근거 수준과 한계를 함께 설명하는”

These alternatives still require verification and regulatory review in commercial contexts.

### “Doctor-Developed”

May describe genuine development involvement, but it does not prove efficacy.

Clarify:

- The physician’s actual role
- Whether formulation was independently tested
- Whether the final product has clinical evidence
- Conflicts of interest

## 27. Functional Medicine and “Root Cause” Claims

Functional medicine terminology must not be used to imply certainty beyond evidence.

High-risk terms include:

- Root cause
- Detox
- Toxin removal
- Adrenal fatigue
- Leaky gut
- Neuroinflammation
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Hormonal imbalance
- Hidden inflammation
- Brain detox

Before public use:

- Define the term precisely
- Identify whether it is an accepted diagnosis, biomarker, mechanism, or hypothesis
- State evidence limitations
- Avoid universal testing recommendations
- Avoid guaranteed causation
- Avoid replacing established diagnosis or treatment

Preferred approach:

> “여러 생활습관·영양·수면·대사 요인을 함께 살펴보는 관점”

Avoid:

> “검사 하나로 뇌 증상의 근본 원인을 찾습니다.”

---

## 28. Visual, Audio, and Implied Claims

Claims can be communicated without words.

High-risk visual or audio elements include:

- Diseased brain becoming normal after product use
- Plaque dissolving
- Neurons regenerating
- Tremor disappearing
- Brain scan normalization
- Prescription medicine imagery
- Hospital monitors
- White coats
- Medical seals
- Dramatic “before/after” transitions
- Alarm sounds
- Fear-based patient scenes
- Graphs without valid scales or denominators

Conceptual illustrations must be labeled when necessary.

Do not use a scientific-looking image to imply a clinical result that has not been established.

---

## 29. Graphs, Statistics, and Numerical Claims

Every numerical claim must have:

- Source
- Population
- Sample size
- Time period
- Outcome definition
- Denominator
- Comparator
- Uncertainty where material
- Access date
- Context

Do not:

- Use relative risk alone when absolute risk is needed
- Truncate axes to exaggerate differences
- Combine incomparable studies
- Use percentages without denominators
- Present subgroup results as general effects
- Use laboratory values as proof of clinical benefit
- Select only favorable time points
- Use outdated market-share data for current superiority claims

Statistics must be understandable to the intended audience.

---

## 30. Disclaimers

Disclaimers are supplemental controls.

They are not permission to make misleading claims.

A disclaimer cannot cure:

- A prohibited disease claim
- A false statement
- A misleading overall impression
- Hidden commercial content
- A fabricated testimonial
- An unauthorized medical advertisement
- A product-category misrepresentation

Disclaimers must be:

- Clear
- Proximate
- Readable
- Audible where relevant
- Consistent with the main message
- Appropriate for the channel

Use only disclaimers that are accurate and applicable.

Do not rely on generic language such as:

- “Results may vary”
- “For information only”
- “Not medical advice”

to justify otherwise misleading content.

---

## 31. Safety, Contraindications, and Material Omissions

Omitting material safety information may make content misleading.

Consider whether the content must address:

- Adverse effects
- Drug interactions
- Allergies
- Pregnancy
- Lactation
- Children
- Older adults
- Liver disease
- Kidney disease
- Bleeding risk
- Surgery
- Dose limits
- Duration
- Duplication with other products
- Need for medical evaluation

The prominence of safety information should be proportional to:

- Risk severity
- Claim strength
- Product accessibility
- Audience vulnerability
- Likelihood of self-treatment

A benefit statement must not be presented so prominently that necessary risk information becomes functionally invisible.

---

## 32. Vulnerable Audiences

Heightened caution is required for content directed to:

- Patients with neurological disease
- People worried about dementia
- Older adults
- Children and adolescents
- Pregnant or lactating individuals
- People with chronic disease
- Caregivers under stress
- People with cognitive impairment
- People taking multiple medicines

Do not exploit fear, urgency, caregiver guilt, or uncertainty.

Do not imply that failure to purchase a product will cause disease or irreversible decline.

---

## 33. Pricing, Discounts, and Urgency

Pricing and promotional claims must be truthful and verifiable.

Do not use:

- False reference prices
- Continuous “limited-time” discounts
- Fake countdown timers
- False scarcity
- Misleading stock claims
- Hidden subscription terms
- Materially unclear shipping or cancellation terms
- Disease fear combined with immediate-purchase pressure

## 35. Privacy, Patient Cases, and Health Information

Patient and consumer health information requires heightened protection.

Before using a case, testimonial, photograph, message, review, recording, imaging study, or laboratory result:

- Identify whether a person can be recognized directly or indirectly
- Minimize data
- Remove unnecessary details
- Obtain valid documented consent where required
- Define permitted channels and duration
- Address withdrawal procedures
- Secure the original data
- Restrict access
- Confirm that publication does not violate professional confidentiality
- Review re-identification risk

Removing a name alone may not be sufficient.

Rare diagnoses, dates, images, occupations, locations, or detailed timelines may identify the person.

AI-generated or composite cases must be labeled as illustrative and must not be presented as real patients.

## 39. Overseas Evidence and Foreign Regulatory References

NervLock’s primary public compliance standard is Korean law unless a separate jurisdiction has been approved.

FDA, EFSA, or another foreign authority may be referenced only when:

- The exact status is verified
- The jurisdiction is stated
- The scope is accurately described
- It is not presented as Korean approval
- The foreign decision is not overstated
- The current status is confirmed

Do not use:

- “FDA approved” for an ingredient, facility, registration, notification, or claim when formal product approval did not occur
- “European approved” without naming the authority and scope
- Foreign evidence to bypass Korean claim restrictions

Separate jurisdiction-specific modules must be created before overseas sales or targeted advertising.

## 44. Prohibited Internal Shortcuts

NervLock teams and AI systems must not assume:

- “It is educational, so advertising law does not apply”
- “There is a paper, so the claim is legal”
- “A doctor said it, so it is allowed”
- “The product is a health functional food, so disease claims are allowed”
- “The wording was approved once, so it can be used everywhere”
- “A disclaimer makes the claim safe”
- “Foreign approval permits Korean advertising”
- “The influencer is responsible, not the brand”
- “A testimonial is only personal opinion”
- “The image contains the claim, not the text”
- “The claim is in a hashtag, so it does not count”
- “The article and product page are separate, so the overall funnel is irrelevant”

All of these assumptions are unsafe.

## 45. Incident and Correction Procedure

When a potentially noncompliant asset is identified:

1. Pause paid promotion
2. Stop further scheduled distribution
3. Preserve the asset and publication record
4. Assess consumer and medical risk
5. Identify affected channels and derivatives
6. Remove or correct the content where appropriate
7. Notify responsible reviewers
8. Determine whether regulator, platform, partner, or consumer notification is required
9. Update claim dossiers and governance documents
10. Record root cause and preventive action

Do not quietly edit a high-risk violation without preserving the original record.

## 48. Pre-Publication Regulatory Checklist

Before publication, confirm:

1. Has the product or service been correctly classified?
2. Has the content type been classified?
3. Is there a commercial nexus?
4. Could this be medical advertising?
5. Are all express claims listed?
6. Are all implied visual and audio claims listed?
7. Is each claim scientifically supported?
8. Is each claim legally permitted for the category?
9. Does disease language appear near a product?
10. Could the product be mistaken for medicine?
11. Could ordinary food be mistaken for health functional food?
12. Is required self-review complete?
13. Is required medical-advertising review complete?
14. Is the published version identical in meaning to the reviewed version?
15. Are safety limitations visible?
16. Are physician credentials accurate and used responsibly?
17. Are testimonials genuine and lawfully usable?
18. Are commercial relationships disclosed?
19. Are comparative claims substantiated?
20. Are certifications described accurately?
21. Are privacy and consent requirements satisfied?
22. Are copyright and image rights secured?
23. Are metadata and hashtags compliant?
24. Are e-commerce notices complete?
25. Are foreign regulatory references precise?
26. Has final human approval been recorded?

If any material answer is unresolved, do not publish.

## 50. Final Regulatory Principles

The overall impression matters more than isolated words.

A disclaimer cannot legalize a prohibited claim.

A paper cannot legalize an unauthorized advertisement.

A physician title cannot replace evidence.

Ingredient evidence cannot automatically prove the finished product.

Foreign approval cannot automatically authorize Korean advertising.

Educational content must not disguise commerce.

Disease fear must not be used to sell products.

Testimonials must not bypass claim restrictions.

Reviewed wording must not become stronger when adapted to short-form media.

When regulatory status is unclear, do not guess.

When a safer accurate alternative exists, use it.

When the risk is material, require specialist and final human review.

NervLock protects long-term trust by publishing only claims that are both scientifically supportable and regulatorily defensible.
