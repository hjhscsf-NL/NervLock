## 1. Purpose

This document defines how NervLock must discover, verify, record, cite, display, update, and audit references.

It applies to all NervLock work involving:

- Medical claims
- Scientific explanations
- Product rationale
- Ingredient evidence
- Safety information
- Regulatory statements
- Clinical guidelines
- Statistics
- Market data
- Website content
- Blog articles
- Social media
- Video scripts
- Newsletters
- Product pages
- Infographics
- Professional education
- Patient education
- Internal strategy
- AI-generated research summaries
- Claude and Claude Code implementation

The purpose of this policy is to prevent:

- Fabricated references
- Incorrect DOI numbers
- Incorrect titles, authors, journals, or publication years
- Citation of retracted research as valid evidence
- Citation of secondary summaries as if they were primary sources
- Claim inflation beyond the cited source
- Citation of an abstract as if full text had been reviewed
- Selective citation of favorable evidence
- Citation laundering
- Misleading use of journal prestige
- Broken, unstable, or unverifiable links
- Loss of traceability between claims and sources
- Copyright infringement through excessive quotation or figure reproduction

References exist to make NervLock’s claims traceable and reviewable.

They are not decorative signals of authority.

---

## 2. Governing Principle

NervLock follows one central reference rule:

> Every material factual claim must be traceable to a real, verified, relevant, and accurately represented source.

A valid citation requires more than a source that appears related.

The source must support the exact statement being made.

Reference quality depends on:

- Existence
- Identity
- Relevance
- Directness
- Methodological quality
- Currency
- Status
- Accessibility
- Accurate interpretation
- Appropriate citation placement

A citation does not correct a misleading sentence.

A real paper does not justify a claim that the paper did not support.

## 3. Scope and Risk Proportionality

The depth of reference verification must match the risk of the content.

### Level 0 — Administrative Content

Examples:

- File names
- Internal task lists
- Non-factual workflow notes

Reference requirement:

- Usually none

### Level 1 — General Educational Content

Examples:

- Basic anatomy
- General lifestyle education
- Stable definitions

Reference requirement:

- Reliable authoritative source
- Primary source where a specific study finding is discussed

### Level 2 — Medical or Scientific Content

Examples:

- Disease mechanisms
- Risk factors
- Symptom interpretation
- Clinical recommendations
- Supplement discussion

Reference requirement:

- Current guideline or high-quality review where appropriate
- Verified primary studies for specific findings
- Safety sources
- Explicit uncertainty

### Level 3 — Product, Regulatory, or High-Stakes Content

Examples:

- Product claim
- Dose rationale
- Contraindication
- Disease-related commercial language
- Medical advertising
- Regulatory statement

Reference requirement:

- Full claim-to-source mapping
- Primary-source verification
- Current official regulatory source
- Retraction and correction check
- Version and access date
- Final human review

No high-risk claim may rely on an unverified citation.

## 4. Reference Roles

Every source should be assigned a role.

### 4.1 Governing Source

Defines an official rule, legal requirement, guideline, or standard.

Examples:

- Statute
- Regulation
- Official notice
- Clinical guideline
- Regulatory authority document

### 4.2 Evidence Source

Provides empirical evidence.

Examples:

- Randomized trial
- Cohort study
- Diagnostic accuracy study
- Systematic review
- Safety study

### 4.3 Context Source

Provides background, terminology, history, or explanation.

Examples:

- Textbook
- Narrative review
- Institutional overview

### 4.4 Discovery Source

Helps locate primary information but is not used as final proof.

Examples:

- Search engine result
- News article
- Press release
- AI answer
- Wikipedia
- Blog post

### 4.5 Commercial Source

Provides product-specific information.

Examples:

- Manufacturer specification
- Certificate of analysis
- Label
- Patent
- Product brochure

Commercial sources may verify product identity or manufacturer claims.

They do not independently establish clinical efficacy.

---

## 5. Source Hierarchy

Source priority depends on the question.

### Medical Recommendations

Prefer:

1. Current rigorous clinical guidelines
2. Current systematic reviews
3. Pivotal primary studies
4. Relevant safety and surveillance sources
5. Expert consensus when higher-level evidence is unavailable

### Specific Study Findings

Prefer:

1. Original full-text primary study
2. Official correction or supplementary material
3. Verified abstract only when full text is unavailable and this limitation is disclosed

### Regulatory Requirements

Prefer:

1. Current statute or regulation
2. Official regulatory notice
3. Official authority guidance
4. Official enforcement or interpretation material
5. Specialist legal interpretation when needed

### Product Identity and Quality

Prefer:

1. Official product label
2. Manufacturer technical specification
3. Certificate of analysis
4. Independent laboratory report
5. Regulatory database entry

### Current Events or Safety Alerts

Prefer:

1. Official regulator or institution
2. Original announcement
3. High-quality news source for context only

A hierarchy is not a substitute for critical appraisal.

A high-ranking source may be outdated, indirect, or methodologically weak.

---

## 6. Primary and Secondary Sources

### Primary Source

A source that directly reports original data, an official decision, a law, a guideline, or an original standard.

Examples:

- Original clinical trial
- Cohort study
- Official guideline
- Statute
- Regulatory notice
- Original dataset
- Official product label

### Secondary Source

A source that summarizes or interprets primary material.

Examples:

- Systematic review
- Narrative review
- Textbook
- News article
- Educational website

Systematic reviews are secondary sources but may be the best starting point for evidence synthesis.

When a specific numerical or methodological claim comes from an original study, cite the original study where feasible.

Do not cite a review as proof that the underlying primary study said something the review did not clearly report.

---

## 7. Minimum Source Eligibility

Before a source is used, confirm:

- The source exists
- The title is correct
- The authors or issuing body are correct
- The publication or issuing date is correct
- The journal, publisher, or authority is correct
- The source can be located by another reviewer
- The source supports the relevant claim
- The source has not been retracted
- Relevant corrections or updates were checked
- The version used is identified
- Conflicts or commercial interests are considered
- The source is appropriate for the audience and purpose

If any essential item is unresolved, label the source `Unverified`.

---

## 8. Prohibited Reference Practices

NervLock and all AI systems must never:

- Invent a citation
- Invent a DOI
- Invent a PMID or PMCID
- Invent an author
- Invent a journal
- Invent a publication year
- Invent an issue or page number
- Invent a guideline
- Alter a title to fit a claim
- Cite a source not accessed
- Claim full-text review when only an abstract was accessed
- Cite a retracted article as valid evidence
- Hide an expression of concern
- Use an unrelated paper because the title sounds relevant
- Cite a reference list without checking the cited source
- Use a review’s citation as proof without checking context
- Copy references generated by AI without verification
- Use a citation to imply stronger evidence than the source provides
- Cite only favorable evidence while concealing material contradictory evidence
- Use journal impact factor as proof of validity
- Use citation count as proof of truth
- Cite a predatory or pseudo-journal without explicit justification
- Reproduce copyrighted figures merely because the paper is cited

## 9. Claim-to-Source Mapping

Every material claim should be linked to one or more sources.

A claim-to-source record should include:

- Claim ID
- Exact claim text
- Intended audience
- Intended channel
- Source ID
- Source type
- Source status
- Exact supporting location
- Evidence level
- Limitations
- Date verified
- Reviewer

The source must support:

- The same population
- The same exposure or intervention
- The same outcome
- The same direction of effect
- A proportionate magnitude
- The same level of certainty
- The same dose and formulation when product-related

A citation to a general mechanism must not support a specific clinical outcome claim.

---

## 10. Citation Placement

A citation should appear close enough to show which statement it supports.

### Preferred

- At the end of the sentence containing the supported claim
- Immediately after a numerical claim
- Immediately after a quoted statement
- At the end of the relevant paragraph when the whole paragraph comes from the same source

### Avoid

- One citation after several unrelated claims
- A reference list without in-text mapping
- Citations placed only in a general “Sources” section when claim attribution is unclear
- Citations separated from the claim by unrelated paragraphs
- A footnote that appears to support more than the source actually covers

When multiple sources support different parts of a sentence, split the sentence or cite each part clearly.

---

## 11. Exact Support Rule

Before citing a source, answer:

1. What exact claim does this source support?
2. Where in the source is the support located?
3. Does the source report data or merely discuss a hypothesis?
4. Is the claim more certain than the source?
5. Is the population the same?
6. Is the intervention, ingredient, dose, and formulation the same?
7. Is the outcome patient-important or surrogate?
8. Is the cited result primary, secondary, exploratory, or subgroup?
9. Are important limitations omitted?
10. Is contrary evidence material?

If the answer cannot be documented, the citation is not ready.

---

## 12. Full Text, Abstract, and Snippet Status

Every scientific source must be classified by access level.

### Full Text Reviewed

The complete article and relevant supplement were accessed.

Permitted statement:

- “Full text reviewed”

### Abstract Reviewed

Only the indexed or publisher abstract was accessed.

Required disclosure:

- “Abstract only; methods and limitations could not be fully verified”

### Metadata Only

Only title, authors, journal, date, and identifier were verified.

Required disclosure:

- “Metadata verified; scientific content not reviewed”

### Search Snippet Only

A search-result excerpt was seen.

Required action:

- Do not use as evidence
- Retrieve the source before citation

A search snippet is not a source.

---

## 13. Bibliographic Metadata Verification

For journal articles, verify metadata using at least one reliable bibliographic record and, for high-risk content, the publisher or original article.

Verify:

- Exact title
- Author list
- Journal title
- Publication year
- Volume
- Issue
- Page range or article number
- DOI
- PMID where available
- Publication type
- Retraction or correction status

For important sources, compare:

- Publisher page
- PubMed
- Crossref metadata
- Journal PDF or HTML

Resolve discrepancies before publication.

---

## 14. DOI Policy

### DOI Verification

A DOI must:

- Exist
- Resolve
- Lead to the intended work
- Match the title, authors, and journal
- Match the version being cited

Do not infer a DOI from formatting patterns.

Do not repair an incomplete DOI by guessing.

### DOI Display

Display DOI links as full HTTPS URLs:

`https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx`

Avoid:

- `doi:10.xxxx/xxxxx`
- `http://dx.doi.org/...`
- An unlinked DOI string when a link is practical

### DOI Limitations

A DOI:

- Identifies a digital object
- Does not prove scientific quality
- Does not prove peer review
- Does not prove that the object is current
- Does not prove absence of correction or retraction

Check the article status separately.

---

## 15. PMID and PMCID Policy

### PMID

A PMID identifies a PubMed record.

It does not guarantee:

- Full-text availability
- High evidence quality
- Peer review quality
- Absence of retraction
- Clinical relevance

### PMCID

A PMCID identifies an article in PubMed Central.

It may be useful for access and quotation verification.

### Use

When available, record:

- DOI
- PMID
- PMCID

The DOI is generally preferred as the persistent public link for a journal article.

PubMed may be used to verify bibliographic data and publication status.

---

## 16. Clinical Guideline References

For every guideline, record:

- Full title
- Issuing organization
- Year
- Version
- Update date
- Target population
- Jurisdiction
- Methodology
- Recommendation strength
- Evidence certainty
- Official URL
- Access date

Use the official issuing organization’s version.

Do not rely solely on:

- News summaries
- Educational slides
- Third-party blog posts
- Search snippets
- Older archived versions

If an update exists, identify whether the older recommendation remains valid.

When guidelines disagree, cite each relevant guideline and explain the difference.

---

## 17. Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

Record:

- Review question
- Search end date
- Protocol or registration
- Included study types
- Number of studies and participants
- Risk-of-bias method
- Certainty assessment
- Main findings
- Heterogeneity
- Limitations
- Funding
- DOI and identifiers
- Update or correction status

Check whether:

- A newer review exists
- Major trials were published after the search date
- The review was withdrawn or corrected
- The conclusion exceeds the underlying evidence

A meta-analysis citation must not be used merely because it appears high in an evidence hierarchy.

---

## 18. Primary Clinical Studies

For important primary studies, record:

- Study design
- Registration number
- Protocol or statistical analysis plan where available
- Population
- Sample size
- Intervention or exposure
- Comparator
- Outcomes
- Follow-up
- Effect estimate
- Confidence interval
- Funding
- Conflicts of interest
- Risk-of-bias concerns
- DOI and identifiers
- Retraction or correction status

Where possible, compare the publication with:

- Trial registry
- Protocol
- Supplement
- Statistical analysis plan

Do not cite exploratory or post hoc findings as prespecified primary evidence.

---

## 19. Trial Registries

Clinical trial registrations may be used to verify:

- Study existence
- Sponsor
- Enrollment
- Intervention
- Primary and secondary outcomes
- Recruitment status
- Dates
- Protocol changes
- Results reporting

A registry record does not by itself prove efficacy.

When a publication is cited, compare major outcomes with the registry when material.

Record the registration number, such as:

- NCT number
- CRIS identifier
- ISRCTN

Use the official registry record.

---

## 20. Protocols and Review Registrations

Protocols and prospective registrations may help identify:

- Prespecified objectives
- Eligibility criteria
- Outcomes
- Analytic plan
- Deviations
- Selective reporting

For systematic reviews, check an appropriate registry such as PROSPERO when applicable.

Registration improves transparency.

It does not guarantee methodological quality or completion.

---

## 21. Preprints

Preprints may be cited only when:

- The topic is emerging and timely
- No adequate peer-reviewed source exists
- The preprint is clearly labeled
- Methods are reviewed
- Claims remain cautious
- Publication status is rechecked before publication
- High-risk clinical or product decisions do not rely solely on the preprint

Required label:

> Preprint; not peer reviewed.

If a peer-reviewed version exists, use the version of record and compare material changes.

---

## 22. Conference Abstracts

Conference abstracts have limited detail.

They may be used to identify emerging evidence but should not support strong claims unless:

- Full methods are available
- The result is clearly labeled preliminary
- The source can be verified
- No full publication exists
- The limitation is explicit

Recheck whether the abstract was later published as a full article.

---

## 23. Narrative Reviews, Textbooks, and Educational Resources

These sources may be useful for:

- Background
- Terminology
- Historical context
- Broad explanation

They should not be the sole support for:

- High-risk product claims
- Specific efficacy claims
- Current regulatory statements
- Novel treatment recommendations

Check the underlying primary sources when the review makes a material claim.

---

## 24. Government and Institutional Websites

Official websites may be used for:

- Laws
- Regulations
- Safety notices
- Public health statistics
- Guidelines
- Definitions
- Product recalls
- Regulatory status

Record:

- Issuing institution
- Page title
- Publication or update date
- Version where available
- URL
- Access date

A government domain does not mean every page is current.

Check the update date and current applicability.

---

## 25. Laws, Regulations, and Regulatory Guidance

For legal or regulatory references, record:

- Full official title
- Article or section
- Jurisdiction
- Effective date
- Amendment date
- Current status
- Official source
- Access date

Prefer:

1. Official legal text
2. Official notice
3. Official regulator guidance

Do not cite an unofficial summary as the sole authority for a high-risk regulatory decision.

When wording is interpretive or uncertain, state that specialist review is required.

---

## 26. Product, Manufacturer, and Commercial Sources

Commercial sources may verify:

- Ingredient identity
- Product composition
- Manufacturing process
- Certificate
- Label
- Quality specification
- Company claim

They do not independently prove:

- Clinical efficacy
- Superiority
- Safety in all populations
- Disease benefit
- Regulatory permission

Mark commercial sources clearly.

Where possible, verify important claims using independent sources.

---

## 27. News, Press Releases, Blogs, and Social Media

These sources may be used for:

- Discovery
- Public reaction
- Current event context
- Identifying an original announcement

They should not be the final evidence source for medical claims when primary sources are available.

### Press Release Rule

A press release may accurately describe an announcement but is promotional material.

Retrieve:

- The original paper
- Trial record
- Regulatory decision
- Official dataset

before making a scientific claim.

### Social Media Rule

A post may document what a person or organization publicly said.

It does not prove the statement is accurate.

Archive the date and account identity when material.

---

## 28. AI-Generated References

AI output is never accepted as a verified bibliography by default.

Every AI-generated citation must be independently checked for:

- Existence
- Correct title
- Correct authors
- Correct journal
- Correct year
- Correct DOI
- Correct PMID
- Correct source type
- Relevant content
- Retraction or correction status

AI must not “complete” missing metadata by prediction.

When verification fails:

- Remove the citation
- Mark the claim unsupported
- Search for a real source
- Do not preserve a fabricated citation as a placeholder

---

## 29. Citation Laundering

Citation laundering occurs when an unsupported claim appears credible because multiple secondary sources repeat it.

Warning signs include:

- Many articles cite the same review
- The review cites another review
- The original source does not support the claim
- The claim changed as it moved across sources
- A press release became the basis of medical content
- A mechanism became a clinical outcome through repetition

For high-impact claims, trace the citation chain back to the primary source.

Do not assume repetition equals validation.

---

## 30. Retractions, Expressions of Concern, and Corrections

Before using an important scientific source, check:

- Publisher page
- PubMed linked notices
- Crossmark where available
- Crossref metadata
- Retraction databases when appropriate

### Retracted Article

Do not cite as valid evidence.

It may be cited only to discuss:

- The retraction
- Research integrity
- Historical impact

Clearly label it as retracted.

### Expression of Concern

Treat as materially uncertain.

Do not use as the sole support for high-risk claims.

Escalate for specialist review.

### Correction

Read the correction and determine whether it changes:

- Data
- Results
- Conclusions
- Authorship
- Methods
- Interpretation

Use the corrected version and document the change.

### Withdrawal or Removal

Determine the reason and whether the content remains valid or accessible.

Do not cite unavailable or withdrawn material without explaining status.

---

## 31. Version of Record

Prefer the final version of record when available.

Distinguish among:

- Preprint
- Accepted manuscript
- Early online version
- Version of record
- Corrected version
- Updated version
- Retracted version

When versions differ materially:

- Use the current authoritative version
- Record the version used
- Explain relevant differences
- Update derivative content

Do not cite a preprint when a materially updated peer-reviewed version exists unless comparing versions.

---

## 32. Duplicate, Overlapping, and Salami Publications

Multiple papers may report overlapping data.

Before treating them as independent evidence, compare:

- Study population
- Recruitment dates
- Sites
- Sample size
- Intervention
- Outcomes
- Authors
- Trial registration

Do not count the same participants multiple times.

Prefer the most complete report while citing companion papers when necessary.

---

## 33. Journal and Publisher Evaluation

Journal reputation may inform caution but does not determine truth.

Assess:

- Indexing
- Editorial transparency
- Peer-review information
- Correction and retraction policy
- Conflict-of-interest disclosure
- Publication ethics
- Contact information
- Suspicious solicitation
- Unrealistic publication speed
- Scope and editorial board authenticity

Avoid predatory or pseudo-journals.

If no alternative source exists and the material is important, label the limitation and require expert review.

---

## 34. Funding and Conflicts of Interest

For material scientific sources, record:

- Funding source
- Sponsor role
- Author employment
- Consulting
- Patents
- Equity
- Data ownership
- Analysis control
- Publication control

Industry funding does not automatically invalidate a study.

It requires transparent reporting and closer scrutiny.

Do not omit a material conflict because the study supports a desired claim.

---

## 35. Reference Currency

Current evidence must be verified for topics likely to change.

Examples:

- Clinical guidelines
- Regulatory rules
- Product recalls
- Safety warnings
- Diagnostic criteria
- Treatment recommendations
- Platform policies
- Market statistics

Record:

- Search date
- Evidence cutoff date
- Access date
- Planned re-review date

A classic older source may remain valid.

Recency alone does not determine quality.

---

## 36. Reference Balance

A reference set should represent the relevant evidence, not merely favorable evidence.

Include when material:

- Supporting evidence
- Neutral evidence
- Contradictory evidence
- Safety evidence
- Guideline disagreement
- Evidence limitations

Do not create false balance when one side is clearly weaker.

Do not create false consensus by omitting credible disagreement.

---

## 37. Numerical Claims

Every numerical claim must identify:

- Source
- Population
- Sample size
- Time period
- Outcome definition
- Denominator
- Comparator
- Measure
- Uncertainty
- Relevant limitation

Do not cite a percentage without verifying the denominator and context.

Do not transfer a statistic from one population to another without qualification.

---

## 38. Quotations and Paraphrases

### Direct Quotations

Use sparingly.

Verify the exact wording in the source.

Record:

- Page
- Section
- Paragraph
- Table
- Figure
- Timestamp

Do not alter meaning with ellipses or selective fragments.

### Paraphrases

A paraphrase must preserve:

- Meaning
- Certainty
- Scope
- Population
- Limitations

Paraphrasing does not remove the need for citation.

Do not produce a close imitation of copyrighted wording.

---

## 39. Copyright and Source Reuse

Citation and copyright permission are separate issues.

A citation does not automatically permit reproduction of:

- Figures
- Tables
- Photographs
- Infographics
- Extensive text
- Video
- Audio
- Questionnaires
- Scales

Before reuse, confirm:

- License
- Permission
- Attribution requirement
- Adaptation rights
- Commercial-use rights
- Share-alike conditions

Prefer original diagrams and summaries.

When adapting permitted material, state that it is adapted and preserve scientific meaning.

## 40. NervLock Citation Style

### 40.1 Medical and Scientific References

Default house style:

- Numbered references
- NLM/Vancouver-compatible order and core metadata
- DOI displayed as a full HTTPS URL
- PMID or PMCID added when useful
- Access date for dynamic web sources

### 40.2 Journal Article Format

`Author(s). Article title. Journal title. Year;Volume(Issue):Pages or Article Number. https://doi.org/...`

Optional:

`PMID: ...; PMCID: ...`

### 40.3 Guideline Format

`Issuing Organization. Guideline Title. Version. Publication Year. Official URL. Accessed YYYY-MM-DD.`

### 40.4 Web Source Format

`Organization. Page Title. Published or Updated Date. URL. Accessed YYYY-MM-DD.`

### 40.5 Law or Regulation Format

`Jurisdiction. Official Title. Article/Section. Effective or Amended Date. Official URL. Accessed YYYY-MM-DD.`

Formatting consistency is required, but bibliographic accuracy takes priority over visual uniformity.

---

## 41. Public-Facing Reference Display

### Long-Form Articles

Include:

- In-text numbered citations
- Full references
- Relevant guideline and source dates
- Last medical review date

### Product Pages

Include only references that:

- Directly support the exact discussion
- Do not create unauthorized claims
- Are understandable in context
- Have passed regulatory review

A bibliography must not be used to imply that the finished product is clinically proven.

### Social Media

Use:

- Concise source line
- Link in caption, comments, profile, or linked source page where practical
- Clear evidence qualification

Do not place a vague “studies show” statement without a retrievable source.

### Video

Include:

- Sources in description or linked source page
- On-screen source for major numerical or high-risk claims
- Access date where relevant

A spoken claim must not become untraceable after publication.

## 43. Reference Verification Workflow

### Step 1. Define the Claim

Write the exact claim before searching.

### Step 2. Select the Best Source Type

Match the source to the question.

### Step 3. Retrieve the Source

Obtain:

- Official source
- Publisher page
- Full text where possible
- Supplement
- Registry
- Correction notice

### Step 4. Verify Identity

Confirm:

- Title
- Author
- Journal or authority
- Date
- DOI or identifier

### Step 5. Verify Status

Check:

- Retraction
- Expression of concern
- Correction
- Updated version

### Step 6. Verify Support

Locate the exact supporting text or data.

### Step 7. Appraise Quality

Apply `03_Medical_Evidence_Standard.md`.

### Step 8. Check Regulatory Use

Apply `04_Regulatory_Compliance.md`.

### Step 9. Record the Source

Create the source record and Claim ID link.

### Step 10. Cite and Audit

Insert the citation, then verify that the final wording remains proportionate.

## 44. Minimum Verification Checklist

Before a reference is approved, confirm:

1. Does the source exist?
2. Is the title correct?
3. Are the authors or issuing body correct?
4. Is the publication date correct?
5. Is the journal, publisher, or authority correct?
6. Does the DOI resolve to the intended work?
7. Are PMID and PMCID accurate where used?
8. Was the full text reviewed?
9. If not, is the limitation disclosed?
10. Does the source support the exact claim?
11. Is the population relevant?
12. Is the dose or formulation relevant?
13. Is the outcome relevant?
14. Is the certainty represented correctly?
15. Were important limitations retained?
16. Was retraction status checked?
17. Were corrections or updates checked?
18. Was a newer authoritative source considered?
19. Are conflicts of interest recorded?
20. Is the citation placed clearly?
21. Is the reference formatted accurately?
22. Is copyright use permitted?
23. Does public use require regulatory review?
24. Has a human reviewer approved high-risk use?

If a material item is unresolved, the reference is not approved.

## 45. Source Status Labels

### Verified — Full Text

Identity, status, and full content reviewed.

### Verified — Abstract Only

Identity and abstract reviewed; full methods not verified.

### Verified — Metadata Only

Bibliographic existence verified; content not reviewed.

### Current Official Source

Official current version confirmed.

### Outdated

A newer version or superseding source exists.

### Corrected

A correction exists and has been reviewed.

### Expression of Concern

Integrity concern exists; use restricted.

### Retracted

Do not use as valid evidence.

### Unverified

One or more essential details remain unresolved.

### Not Suitable

Real source, but not appropriate for the claim.

## 46. Reference Audit

High-risk content should undergo an independent reference audit.

The audit should confirm:

- Every material claim has a source
- Every citation exists
- Every identifier is accurate
- Every citation supports the claim
- No retracted source is used as valid evidence
- Corrections are incorporated
- Source access level is honest
- Evidence balance is appropriate
- Regulatory interpretation uses official sources
- Product claims match exact evidence
- The final wording did not strengthen after source review

The drafter should not be the only verifier for Level 3 content.

---

## 47. Correction and Withdrawal Procedure

When a reference error is identified:

1. Preserve the original content
2. Determine affected claims
3. Determine affected derivative content
4. Correct the citation
5. Correct or remove the claim
6. Check whether safety or regulatory risk exists
7. Update the source record
8. Update Claim IDs
9. Notify relevant reviewers
10. Publish a correction when material
11. Recheck all reused instances

A citation correction is insufficient if the underlying claim is also wrong.

---

## 48. Archiving and Reproducibility

For major evidence and regulatory work, archive:

- Search strategy
- Search date
- Database
- Search results
- Included sources
- Excluded key sources and reasons
- Full citations
- Accessible files or lawful links
- Evidence tables
- Risk-of-bias notes
- Source status
- Claim-to-source mapping
- Final content version
- Review approval

Do not archive copyrighted full text in a way that violates licensing.

Preserve enough information for another reviewer to reproduce the search and verify the decision.

## 49. AI Reference Rules

AI may assist with:

- Search-term development
- Literature discovery
- Metadata extraction
- Citation formatting
- DOI lookup
- Evidence-table drafting
- Claim-to-source mapping
- Duplicate detection
- Retraction-check prompts
- Reference audits

AI must not:

- Create a source from memory
- Guess missing metadata
- Treat a search snippet as evidence
- State that a paper was reviewed when it was not
- Generate an unsupported DOI
- Cite a paper because another AI cited it
- Hide failed verification
- Select only favorable references
- Approve its own high-risk bibliography

AI output must distinguish:

- Found
- Opened
- Full text reviewed
- Abstract reviewed
- Metadata verified
- Not verified

## 51. Review Triggers

Immediate reference re-review is required when:

- A source is corrected
- A source is retracted
- An expression of concern appears

## 52. Final Reference Principles

A citation is not decoration.

A DOI is not proof of quality.

A PMID is not proof of truth.

Peer review is not a guarantee.

A meta-analysis is not automatically definitive.

A guideline is not automatically current.

A press release is not a clinical study.

An abstract is not a full-text review.

A mechanism is not a clinical outcome.

A commercial source is not independent proof.

A real paper cannot support a claim it did not make.

A retracted article must not remain hidden in the evidence base.

When a source cannot be verified, say so.

When the source is indirect, qualify the claim.

When the source is outdated, update it.

When evidence conflicts, show the conflict.

When a citation error is found, correct the claim as well as the citation.

NervLock’s credibility depends not on the number of references it displays, but on the accuracy, relevance, transparency, and integrity of every reference it uses.
