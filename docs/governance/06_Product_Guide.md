## 1. Purpose

This document defines how NervLock must identify, evaluate, develop, manufacture, position, launch, monitor, revise, and discontinue products.

It applies to:

- Health functional foods
- Functionally labeled ordinary foods
- General foods
- Nutritional products
- Supplement-like products
- Product bundles
- Educational kits
- Digital or physical brain health tools
- Future products within the NervLock ecosystem

This guide is intended to prevent products from being developed primarily because:

- An ingredient is fashionable
- A competitor is selling it
- A mechanism sounds persuasive
- A supplier proposes a ready-made formula
- A claim appears commercially attractive
- A high dose looks impressive
- A physician title may improve conversion

Every NervLock product must arise from a legitimate consumer need, a transparent scientific rationale, a feasible regulatory pathway, and an acceptable safety and quality profile.

A product is not approved merely because its individual ingredients are legal or supported by some evidence.

---

## 2. Governing Product Principle

NervLock follows one central product rule:

> A product must earn its place in the ecosystem through need, evidence, safety, quality, transparency, and regulatory defensibility.

Products are subordinate to the NervLock mission.

They must not replace or overshadow:

- Education
- Scientific interpretation
- Appropriate medical care
- Sustainable lifestyle practices
- Long-term public trust

Product development must follow the NervLock strategic sequence:

**Build Education**  
↓  
**Earn Trust**  
↓  
**Develop Credibility**  
↓  
**Build Community**  
↓  
**Introduce Evidence-Informed Products**  
↓  
**Strengthen Loyalty**  
↓  
**Expand the Ecosystem**

Products must never become the primary identity of NervLock.

## 4. NervLock Product Philosophy

### 4.1 Need Before Ingredient

Begin with a clearly defined consumer or nutritional need.

Do not begin with a fashionable ingredient and construct a need around it.

### 4.2 Evidence Before Formulation

Evaluate the relevant evidence before selecting ingredients, doses, forms, or combinations.

### 4.3 Safety Before Differentiation

A differentiated formula is not valuable if it creates avoidable safety, interaction, quality, or adherence problems.

### 4.4 Transparency Before Proprietary Mystique

NervLock should explain:

- Why each ingredient is included
- What form is used
- What dose is provided
- What evidence supports it
- What limitations remain
- Who should use caution

Do not obscure meaningful doses behind a proprietary blend unless a lawful and compelling reason exists and full internal transparency is preserved.

### 4.5 Simplicity Before Ingredient Count

More ingredients do not automatically create a better product.

Every ingredient adds:

- Safety considerations
- Interaction potential
- Stability risk
- Manufacturing complexity
- Quality-control burden
- Cost
- Claim risk
- Consumer confusion

### 4.6 Product Quality Before Launch Speed

Do not launch before:

- Formula lock
- Specification approval
- Required testing
- Stability justification
- Label review
- Claim review
- Manufacturing readiness
- Complaint and adverse-event systems

### 4.7 Long-Term Trust Before Short-Term Sales

A product that requires exaggerated claims, fear-based messaging, or physician-authority pressure is not compatible with NervLock.

## 6. Non-Negotiable Product Gates

A product may proceed only if it passes all applicable gates.

### Gate 1 — Strategic Fit

- Does it belong within brain health?
- Does it strengthen the NervLock ecosystem?
- Is it educationally coherent?
- Would it remain valuable without exaggerated marketing?

### Gate 2 — Defined Need

- Is the intended need clear?
- Is the need meaningful?
- Is a product an appropriate response?
- Could lifestyle or medical care be more appropriate?

### Gate 3 — Evidence

- Is there relevant human evidence?
- Does the evidence apply to the target population?
- Does the proposed dose and form match the evidence?
- Are benefits clinically meaningful?

### Gate 4 — Safety

- Are adverse effects understood?
- Are interactions assessed?
- Are vulnerable populations addressed?
- Is long-term uncertainty acceptable?

### Gate 5 — Regulatory Feasibility

- Is the category lawful?
- Are the ingredients permitted?
- Is the intended function authorized?
- Can the product be communicated without prohibited claims?

### Gate 6 — Quality and Manufacturing

- Is a qualified manufacturer available?
- Can identity, purity, potency, and stability be controlled?
- Are specifications and test methods adequate?

### Gate 7 — Commercial Sustainability

- Is the product economically viable without quality compromise?
- Is pricing compatible with long-term trust?
- Can the supply chain be maintained?

### Gate 8 — Operational Readiness

- Are label, customer support, complaint, adverse-event, and recall systems ready?

Failure at any material gate pauses development.

Commercial enthusiasm must not override a failed safety, evidence, quality, or regulatory gate.

## 9. Need Definition

The consumer need must be stated without converting a disease into a product opportunity.

### Appropriate Need Types

- Defined nutritional insufficiency or vulnerability
- Support of an authorized normal physiological function
- Convenience or adherence problem
- Difficulty obtaining a nutrient through usual diet
- Age- or lifestyle-related nutritional consideration
- Need for a transparent, well-controlled formulation

### Inappropriate Need Types for Food or Supplement Positioning

- Treating Parkinson’s disease
- Preventing dementia
- Reversing neurodegeneration
- Replacing prescribed medicine
- Diagnosing the cause of brain fog
- Repairing damaged neurons
- Guaranteeing cognitive enhancement

Disease burden may inform educational strategy.

It must not automatically become a commercial product indication.

## 12. Evidence Development Pathway

Product evidence must be built in layers.

### Layer 1 — Need Evidence

Is the intended need real and relevant in the target population?

### Layer 2 — Ingredient Evidence

Does each ingredient have a valid scientific rationale?

### Layer 3 — Dose and Form Evidence

Does the proposed form and dose match the evidence?

### Layer 4 — Combination Evidence

Are there known or plausible interactions among ingredients?

### Layer 5 — Finished-Product Evidence

Does the exact final product have evidence?

### Layer 6 — Claim Evidence

Does the evidence support the exact intended wording?

### Layer 7 — Regulatory Permission

Is the wording legally permitted for the product category?

Ingredient evidence must not be presented as finished-product evidence.

Mechanistic evidence must not be presented as clinical efficacy.

## 13. Ingredient Selection Criteria

An ingredient may be considered only after review of:

- Legal status
- Identity
- Source
- Chemical or botanical form
- Standardization
- Human evidence
- Dose
- Bioavailability
- Safety
- Interactions
- Stability
- Manufacturing feasibility
- Sensory impact
- Cost
- Sustainability
- Supply reliability
- Regulatory function
- Brand fit

### Preferred Ingredient Characteristics

- Defined identity
- Reproducible composition
- Relevant human evidence
- Dose compatible with the product format
- Acceptable safety margin
- Reliable supply
- Validated testing
- Stable formulation
- Transparent rationale

### Reasons to Reject an Ingredient

- Identity cannot be assured
- Evidence is primarily in vitro or animal
- Human dose cannot fit the product
- Safety is uncertain
- Interaction risk is unacceptable
- Source quality is inconsistent
- Marketing value exceeds scientific value
- Claim requires disease language
- The ingredient is present only for label appeal
- Cost requires underdosing
- Testing cannot adequately verify quality

## 15. Dose Selection

Dose selection must consider:

- Effective dose in human studies
- Range of studied doses
- Duration
- Target population
- Baseline nutritional status
- Tolerable upper intake or safety boundary
- Regulatory daily intake
- Product category
- Bioavailability
- Food effects
- Formulation
- Total exposure from diet and other products
- Adherence
- Capsule or serving burden
- Cost

### Dose Rules

- Do not underdose merely to list an ingredient
- Do not use the highest possible dose as a marketing strategy
- Do not assume more is better
- Do not extrapolate linearly beyond studied doses
- Do not combine multiple sources without calculating total exposure
- Do not ignore vulnerable populations
- Do not convert an acute study dose into indefinite daily use without review

Every dose decision must be documented.

## 16. Formulation Principles

A NervLock formulation should be:

- Purposeful
- Minimal
- Scientifically coherent
- Manufacturable
- Stable
- Safe
- Transparent
- Adherence-friendly

### Formula Inclusion Test

Every ingredient must answer:

1. What defined need does it address?
2. What evidence supports inclusion?
3. What dose is required?
4. Does the final dose match the evidence?
5. Does it create interaction or stability risk?
6. Does it improve the product enough to justify complexity?
7. Can its presence be explained honestly?

If the answer is weak, remove the ingredient.

## 17. Combination and Synergy

“Synergy” is a high-risk scientific and marketing concept.

Do not claim synergy unless supported by evidence for:

- The exact combination
- Relevant doses
- Relevant population
- Appropriate outcome
- Appropriate comparator
- Acceptable study quality

The presence of complementary mechanisms does not prove synergy.

Possible combination effects include:

- Additive benefit
- No additional benefit
- Reduced absorption
- Increased toxicity
- Competing metabolism
- Sensory or stability problems

Use:

- “complementary rationale”
- “designed to address more than one relevant pathway”

only when scientifically accurate and regulatorily acceptable.

Do not use:

- “clinically synergistic”
- “multiplies the effect”
- “works exponentially”

without direct evidence and approval.

---

## 18. Proprietary Blends

NervLock should generally avoid proprietary blends that hide individual doses.

If a proprietary blend is considered:

- Internal ingredient doses must remain fully documented
- Safety assessment must use exact doses
- Manufacturing specifications must define exact composition
- Regulatory requirements must be met
- Consumer transparency must be evaluated
- The blend must not be used to conceal underdosing
- Scientific claims must match the actual composition

A branded ingredient is not automatically superior to a well-characterized generic ingredient.

Evaluate the underlying evidence, identity, quality, and dose.

## 19. Excipients and Additives

Excipients must be selected deliberately.

Evaluate:

- Legal use
- Function
- Daily exposure
- Allergy
- Intolerance
- Stability
- Interaction
- Taste
- Color
- Consumer perception
- Label requirements

Examples include:

- Fillers
- Binders
- Coatings
- Lubricants
- Sweeteners
- Flavors
- Colors
- Preservatives
- Emulsifiers
- Capsule materials
- Anti-caking agents

“Clean label” is not a substitute for product safety, stability, or manufacturability.

Avoid demonizing lawful additives without evidence.

---

## 20. Bioavailability

Bioavailability claims require direct support.

Evaluate:

- Chemical form
- Delivery system
- Food effect
- Solubility
- Particle size
- Metabolism
- Transport
- Co-ingested ingredients
- Study population
- Measured exposure
- Clinical relevance

Higher blood concentration does not automatically mean greater clinical benefit.

Do not claim:

- Superior absorption
- Maximum bioavailability
- Better brain delivery
- Crosses the blood–brain barrier

without relevant verified evidence and regulatory review.

## 29. Test Methods

Test methods should be:

- Appropriate
- Specific
- Accurate
- Precise
- Reproducible
- Sensitive enough
- Suitable for the matrix
- Current
- Documented

Compendial or official methods are preferred when applicable.

Nonstandard methods require appropriate validation or verification.

A method that works for raw material may not work for a complex finished product.

## 30. Identity, Purity, Potency, and Contaminants

At minimum, quality control must consider:

### Identity

Is the material what it claims to be?

### Purity

Is it free from unacceptable impurities or adulterants?

### Potency

Does it contain the intended amount throughout shelf life?

### Contaminants

Consider:

- Heavy metals
- Pesticides
- Residual solvents
- Microorganisms
- Mycotoxins
- Allergens
- Undeclared medicines
- Economically motivated adulteration
- Environmental contaminants
- Oxidation products

Testing frequency must be risk-based.

## 32. Batch Release

No batch should be released until:

- Manufacturing records are complete
- Deviations are resolved
- Test results meet specification
- Packaging is correct
- Label version is correct
- Reconciliation is complete
- Required quality approval is documented
- Distribution traceability is ready

Commercial pressure must not override an unresolved quality investigation.

## 33. Stability and Shelf Life

Shelf life must be justified.

The stability program should consider:

- Real-time conditions
- Accelerated conditions
- Intended packaging
- Temperature
- Humidity
- Light
- Oxygen
- Moisture
- Microbial stability
- Active or marker retention
- Sensory change
- Packaging integrity
- In-use conditions

A supplier’s raw-material shelf life does not establish finished-product shelf life.

Overage must not be used casually to compensate for poor stability.

Any overage requires scientific, quality, and regulatory justification.

- What is this product?

- Who should avoid it?

- What evidence supports it?
- What limitations remain?

- What is the return or complaint process?

FAQs must not introduce claims that are absent from approved labeling or advertising.

- Intake channel

- Seriousness assessment
- Product and lot identification
- Dose and duration
- Concomitant products
- Concomitant medicines
- Medical history
- Outcome
- Follow-up
- Causality assessment
- Current legally applicable reporting deadline
- Regulatory reporting
- Trend review

Scientific, medical, quality, regulatory, brand, UX, commercial, and human approval.

Do not exceed the approved communication territory.

### Stage 15 — Monitor and Improve

Review quality, safety, complaints, evidence, and consumer understanding.

## 55. Bundles and Regimens

Bundles increase interaction and total-exposure risk.

Before creating a bundle:

- Calculate duplicate ingredients
- Review aggregate dose
- Review interactions
- Review adherence
- Define intended duration
- Define target population
- Assess consumer confusion
- Review combined claims
- Review combined price
- Confirm that the bundle does not resemble a disease-treatment protocol

Do not label a consumer bundle as a medical “protocol” unless a lawful professional context applies.

- Ingredient quality
- Manufacturing
- Testing
- Packaging
- Education
- Support
- Sustainable operations

Avoid:

- Fear-based premium pricing
- False reference pricing
- Permanent “limited-time” discounting
- Hidden subscriptions
- Product dependency narratives
- Claims that the most expensive option is medically necessary
- Lowering quality to preserve margin without review

Commercial sustainability matters.

It must remain compatible with consumer trust.

## 57. Discontinuation Criteria

Consider discontinuation when:

- Safety risk becomes unacceptable
- Evidence materially weakens
- Regulatory pathway becomes untenable
- Quality cannot be maintained
- Supplier integrity is compromised
- Product cannot be communicated honestly
- Consumer misunderstanding remains high
- Product no longer fits strategy
- Commercial viability requires unacceptable compromise
- A better non-product solution exists

Discontinuation is not a failure when it protects consumers and long-term trust.

## 61. Final Product Principles

A product is not the brand.

An ingredient is not a product.

A mechanism is not a clinical outcome.

A high dose is not proof of quality.

More ingredients are not automatically better.

A branded ingredient is not automatically superior.

A physician title is not evidence.

A GMP logo is not proof of efficacy.

A certificate of analysis is not proof of clinical benefit.

Ingredient evidence does not automatically validate the finished formula.

A lawful ingredient does not automatically authorize a claim.

A disclaimer does not legalize a prohibited message.

A launch date does not override unresolved safety or quality concerns.

When evidence is limited, the formulation and language must remain conservative.

When quality cannot be verified, do not proceed.

When safety is uncertain, pause.

When the product requires fear or exaggeration to sell, reject the strategy.

NervLock products should make the ecosystem more useful, more transparent, and more trustworthy.

They should never make the brand less scientific.
