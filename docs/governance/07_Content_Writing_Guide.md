## 1. Purpose

This document defines how NervLock content must be planned, written, structured, reviewed, revised, and approved.

It applies to:

- Website copy
- Brain Health Academy content
- Blog articles
- Medical education
- Patient and caregiver education
- Product education
- Product pages
- Frequently asked questions
- Newsletters
- Social media captions
- Infographics
- Video scripts
- YouTube descriptions
- Press materials
- Professional education
- Internal educational documents
- Korean and English content
- AI-generated drafts
- Claude and Claude Code implementation copy

The purpose of this guide is to ensure that all NervLock writing is:

- Scientifically accurate
- Medically responsible
- Clear
- Calm
- Useful
- Transparent
- Audience-appropriate
- Consistent with the brand
- Search-accessible without distortion
- Regulatorily cautious
- Traceable to verified sources
- Reviewed before publication

Writing quality is not measured only by fluency.

A well-written sentence that overstates evidence, creates fear, or implies an unlawful claim is not acceptable NervLock content.

---

## 2. Governing Writing Principle

NervLock follows one central editorial rule:

> Write to improve understanding, not to manufacture certainty.

Every piece of content should help the reader understand:

- What the issue is
- Why it may matter
- What current evidence supports
- What remains uncertain
- What practical action may be reasonable
- When professional evaluation may be appropriate

The reader should feel:

> “I understand this more clearly.”

not:

> “I am frightened and need to buy something immediately.”

---

## 3. Editorial Role of NervLock

NervLock is not a generic wellness publisher.

NervLock is a neurologist-led, evidence-based Brain Health Platform.

Its writing should function as:

- Medical education
- Scientific interpretation
- Public health communication
- Evidence translation
- Practical decision support
- Long-term trust building

NervLock writing should not function as:

- Product-first persuasion
- Fear-based lead generation
- Sensational disease content
- Authority-based pressure
- Trend-driven speculation
- Simplified mechanism marketing
- Hidden advertising
- Unqualified personal medical advice

---

## 4. Writing Priorities

When priorities conflict, use the following order:

1. Public and patient safety
2. Scientific accuracy
3. Regulatory compliance
4. Reference integrity
5. Brand consistency
6. Reader comprehension
7. Practical usefulness
8. Search visibility
9. Engagement
10. Conversion

SEO, click-through rate, watch time, saves, shares, and sales must never override safety or accuracy.

## 5. Audience Definition Before Writing

No substantive content should be drafted before the audience is defined.

### 5.1 General Brain Health Audience

Typical readers:

- Adults interested in long-term brain health
- People concerned about memory, focus, sleep, stress, or fatigue
- Health-conscious consumers
- Middle-aged and older adults
- Readers skeptical of exaggerated wellness claims

Needs:

- Clear explanations
- Realistic action
- Evidence context
- Appropriate reassurance
- Medical red flags

### 5.2 Clinical Education Audience

Typical readers:

- Patients
- Families
- Caregivers
- People with neurological symptoms
- People seeking disease education

Needs:

- Accurate disease information
- Clear medical boundaries
- Warning signs
- Guidance on when to seek care
- Distinction between education and individualized treatment

### 5.3 Professional Audience

Typical readers:

- Physicians
- Healthcare professionals
- Medical students
- Researchers
- Health educators
- Science communicators

Needs:

- Methodological detail
- Evidence grading
- Clinical context
- Precise terminology
- Full references
- Limitations

### 5.4 Product Education Audience

Typical readers:

- Consumers evaluating a product
- Current customers
- Caregivers purchasing for others
- People comparing formulations

Needs:

- Product identity
- Intended use
- Evidence level
- Dose
- Safety
- Limitations
- Who should avoid it
- Clear distinction from medical care

### 5.5 Internal Audience

Typical readers:

- ChatGPT
- Claude
- Claude Code
- Product developers
- Designers
- Marketing partners
- Regulatory reviewers
- Medical reviewers

Needs:

- Precise instructions
- Decision rules
- Version control
- No promotional ambiguity
- Clear approval status

## 6. Reader Intent

Before writing, define the reader’s primary intent.

Common intents include:

- Learn
- Understand a symptom
- Compare options
- Decide whether to seek care
- Evaluate a supplement
- Understand a study
- Understand a diagnosis
- Support a caregiver
- Verify a claim
- Learn a practical habit
- Find a reliable source

A content asset should not attempt to satisfy every intent at once.

The primary intent determines:

- Depth
- Structure
- Terminology
- CTA
- Disclaimer
- Reference density
- Medical caution
- Product proximity

## 7. Content Purpose Classification

Every asset must be classified as one of the following:

### Pure Education

No product or service promotion.

### Brand Education

Explains NervLock’s mission, standards, or approach.

### Medical Education

Explains symptoms, diseases, diagnosis, treatment, risk, or prognosis.

### Lifestyle Education

Explains sleep, exercise, nutrition, stress, or sustainable health behavior.

### Scientific Interpretation

Explains research, guidelines, evidence quality, or uncertainty.

### Product-Adjacent Education

Discusses a nutritional or functional topic related to a product category without direct promotion.

### Product Education

Explains a specific NervLock product.

### Commercial Content

Includes price, purchase, promotion, discount, or direct conversion.

### Professional Education

Written for healthcare or scientific audiences.

### Internal Governance

Defines strategy, workflow, or standards.

Mixed-purpose content must be treated according to the highest-risk component.

- Sales-driven
- Condescending

## 10. Language Standard

### 10.1 Clear but Not Simplistic

Simplify language, not scientific meaning.

Prefer:

- Shorter sentences
- Defined terminology
- Concrete examples
- Clear transitions
- Explicit uncertainty

Avoid removing:

- Limitations
- Population boundaries
- Safety concerns
- Distinction between mechanism and outcome

### 10.2 Professional but Not Academic

Avoid unnecessary jargon.

When a technical term is necessary:

1. Use the correct term
2. Define it in plain language
3. Explain why it matters

### 10.3 Confident but Not Absolute

Use firm language only when evidence is strong and applicable.

Avoid:

- Always
- Never, except for genuine prohibitions
- Guaranteed
- Completely proven
- Works for everyone
- No side effects
- Safe for all

### 10.4 Calm but Not Passive

When medical evaluation is needed, state it clearly.

Do not soften urgent guidance until it becomes ambiguous.

## 11. Korean Writing Principles

Korean NervLock content should:

- Use natural, professional Korean
- Avoid excessive translation-style syntax
- Avoid unnecessary English terms
- Use Korean medical terminology first where practical
- Add English terms in parentheses only when useful
- Avoid excessive honorific burden
- Use respectful but direct language
- Prefer clear sentence endings
- Maintain consistent terminology

### Preferred Korean Style

- “현재 근거는 제한적입니다.”
- “이 증상만으로 특정 질환을 진단할 수는 없습니다.”
- “다음과 같은 경우에는 의료진의 평가가 필요할 수 있습니다.”
- “일부 연구에서 관련성이 보고되었지만 인과관계는 확립되지 않았습니다.”
- “이 성분이 이러한 경로에 관여한다는 사실과 실제 임상적 효과는 구분해야 합니다.”

### Avoid

- “무조건”
- “100%”
- “기적”
- “완치”
- “싹 사라집니다”
- “당장 드셔야 합니다”
- “의사들도 모르는”
- “충격적인 진실”
- “이것만 먹으면”
- “몸이 보내는 경고”

## 12. English Writing Principles

English NervLock content should:

- Use clear international English
- Avoid inflated marketing language
- Avoid idioms that reduce clarity
- Use medically accurate terminology
- Preserve evidence qualifiers
- Avoid direct translation from Korean when unnatural
- Use consistent American or international style within a document

Preferred patterns:

- “Current evidence suggests…”
- “The clinical significance remains uncertain.”
- “This association does not establish causation.”
- “The findings may not apply to all populations.”
- “This information is educational and does not replace individualized medical care.”

Avoid:

- “Miracle”
- “Ultimate”
- “Breakthrough” without strong justification
- “Reverses brain aging”
- “Doctor-approved” as an efficacy claim
- “Science proves”
- “Guaranteed results”

## 13. Terminology Control

NervLock must maintain a controlled vocabulary.

### 13.1 Use Precise Terms

Distinguish:

- Risk factor
- Association
- Cause
- Symptom
- Sign
- Diagnosis
- Prognosis
- Biomarker
- Mechanism
- Clinical outcome
- Surrogate outcome
- Treatment
- Prevention
- Support
- Maintenance

### 13.2 Avoid Uncontrolled Terms

Use caution with:

- Brain detox
- Neuroinflammation
- Root cause
- Adrenal fatigue
- Leaky gut
- Hormone imbalance
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Toxin accumulation
- Brain optimization
- Biohacking

When used, define:

- What the term means
- Whether it is established or hypothetical
- What evidence supports it
- What it does not mean

### 13.3 Disease Names

Use accepted medical terminology.

Do not use disease names as product hooks.

Do not convert ordinary symptoms into disease labels.

---

## 14. Evidence Language

The strength of language must match the evidence.

### Established or High-Certainty Evidence

Use:

- Demonstrates
- Reduces
- Increases
- Is recommended
- Is consistently associated with

Still specify:

- Population
- Outcome
- Conditions
- Magnitude where relevant

### Moderate Evidence

Use:

- Probably
- Likely
- Evidence suggests
- Is reasonably supported

### Low Evidence

Use:

- May
- Might
- Limited evidence suggests
- Some studies have reported

### Very Low or Hypothesis-Level Evidence

Use:

- It is uncertain whether
- A hypothesis has been proposed
- Clinical benefit has not been established
- Evidence is insufficient

### Prohibited Inflation

Do not convert:

- “May support” into “Improves”
- “Associated with” into “Prevents”
- “Biologically plausible” into “Proven”
- “Changes a biomarker” into “Protects the brain”
- “One small study” into “Research confirms”
- “Ingredient evidence” into “This product works”

---

## 15. Writing About Mechanisms

Mechanisms can improve understanding.

They can also create false certainty.

When explaining a mechanism:

1. State whether the evidence is human, animal, cellular, or theoretical
2. Explain the pathway clearly
3. State whether clinical benefit has been demonstrated
4. Avoid implying direct causality
5. Avoid dramatic biological imagery
6. Avoid using mechanism as proof of product efficacy

Preferred structure:

> “This ingredient participates in [pathway]. However, participation in a biological pathway does not by itself establish a meaningful clinical benefit.”

---

## 16. Writing About Association and Causation

Observational associations must not be written as causes.

Preferred:

- “was associated with”
- “was linked to”
- “people with X had a higher observed rate of Y”
- “the study cannot establish causation”

Avoid:

- “X causes Y”
- “X prevents Y”
- “X protects against Y”

unless causation is supported by appropriate evidence.

---

## 17. Writing About Risk

Risk communication must include context.

When possible, explain:

- Baseline risk
- Absolute risk
- Relative risk
- Time frame
- Population
- Uncertainty
- Modifiable versus nonmodifiable factors

Avoid presenting a relative increase without absolute context when that could mislead.

Do not use risk to frighten readers into purchase.

---

## 18. Writing About Statistics

Every statistic should answer:

- Who was studied?
- How many people?
- Over what period?
- Compared with what?
- What outcome?
- What uncertainty?
- Is the difference clinically important?

Avoid:

- Percentages without denominators
- Relative risk without context
- Truncated graphs
- Unsupported “X times higher” claims
- Outdated prevalence data
- Generalizing one population to all readers

Numbers should improve understanding, not create authority theater.

---

## 19. Writing About Symptoms

Symptom content must avoid:

- Self-diagnosis
- Disease certainty
- Product-driven explanations
- Overly broad differential claims
- Minimizing red flags

A symptom article should generally include:

1. What the symptom means
2. Common and important causes
3. Relevant context
4. Self-care boundaries
5. Red flags
6. When to seek medical evaluation
7. What not to assume

Preferred language:

> “This symptom has many possible causes. The pattern, duration, associated symptoms, medication use, sleep, stress, and medical history all matter.”

---

## 20. Writing About Diseases

Disease content should separate:

- Definition
- Symptoms
- Diagnosis
- Risk factors
- Treatment
- Prognosis
- Caregiver issues
- Uncertainty
- When to seek care

Do not use disease education as a direct product funnel.

Avoid:

- “You may have this disease” headlines
- Fear-first openings
- Product placement near urgent warning signs
- Disease-prevention supplement claims
- Suggesting that lifestyle or nutrition replaces standard care

---

## 21. Red-Flag Communication

When red flags are relevant:

- Use direct language
- Name the action
- Avoid false reassurance
- Avoid panic
- Keep the list focused
- Do not overload with rare events

Preferred pattern:

> “다음과 같은 증상이 새롭게 발생했거나 빠르게 악화된다면 즉시 의료기관 평가가 필요할 수 있습니다.”

Red-flag guidance is not a substitute for individualized triage.

---

## 22. Practical Recommendations

Practical advice should be:

- Evidence-aligned
- Feasible
- Specific
- Low-risk
- Appropriate for the audience
- Clear about limitations

Use a hierarchy:

1. Highest-value actions
2. Reasonable optional actions
3. Actions with limited evidence
4. Actions not recommended

Avoid long undifferentiated lists.

A practical recommendation should explain:

- Why it matters
- How to do it
- What a realistic target looks like
- When professional guidance is needed

---

## 23. Lifestyle Content

Lifestyle writing should avoid moral judgment.

Do not imply that illness results from personal failure.

Use:

- Sustainable
- Realistic
- Gradual
- Individualized
- Context-sensitive

Avoid:

- Perfect routines
- Extreme restriction
- Shame-based language
- “No excuses”
- Universal protocols
- Unsupported detox or cleanse plans

---

## 24. Product Writing Principles

Product writing must be more conservative than general educational writing.

Every product page should clearly explain:

- What the product is
- What it contains
- Why ingredients were selected
- What evidence supports the rationale
- What remains uncertain
- Who may consider it
- Who should use caution
- How to use it
- What it does not do
- How it fits with lifestyle and medical care

Product writing must not:

- Use disease fear
- Imply treatment
- Promise prevention
- Guarantee performance
- Hide dose
- Hide limitations
- Use physician credentials as proof
- Use references as decorative authority
- Treat ingredient evidence as finished-product proof

---

## 25. Product Integration in Educational Content

A product should appear only when it adds legitimate educational value.

Before including a product, ask:

1. Is the article complete without the product?
2. Is the product relevant to the exact topic?
3. Is the transition natural?
4. Is the evidence sufficient?
5. Is the product communication lawful?
6. Is the commercial relationship clear?
7. Could the reader mistake education for treatment advice?

Do not add a product recommendation automatically at the end of every article.

---

## 26. Calls to Action

CTAs must match the content purpose.

### Educational CTA

- Read the related guide
- Review the references
- Save this information
- Discuss concerns with a healthcare professional
- Learn about a related topic

### Community CTA

- Subscribe to updates
- Follow for evidence-based education
- Share with a caregiver

### Product CTA

Use only in appropriate commercial context.

Avoid:

- “Buy before it is too late”
- “Protect your brain now”
- “Don’t wait until dementia begins”
- “Everyone over 40 needs this”
- “Your brain cannot recover without it”

A CTA must not create fear, false urgency, or medical pressure.

---

## 27. Headlines and Titles

Headlines should be:

- Accurate
- Specific
- Useful
- Proportionate
- Understandable
- Search-relevant

Avoid:

- Clickbait
- Unsupported certainty
- Fear amplification
- Product-driven disease claims
- “Secret,” “truth,” or “shocking” framing

Preferred title patterns:

- “수면 부족과 기억력: 현재 근거는 무엇을 말할까?”
- “파킨슨병에서 변비가 흔한 이유와 관리 원칙”
- “브레인포그는 질병명일까? 원인과 진료가 필요한 경우”
- “운동과 뇌 건강: 어떤 효과가 비교적 잘 확립되어 있을까?”

Avoid:

- “치매가 시작되고 있다는 7가지 충격 신호”
- “의사들이 숨기는 뇌 영양제”
- “이 성분 하나로 뇌 노화를 멈추세요”

---

## 28. Opening Paragraph

The opening should answer:

- Why this topic matters
- What the reader will learn
- What the article will not claim

Avoid opening with:

- Fear
- Unverified prevalence
- A dramatic anecdote
- Product promotion
- An absolute claim

Preferred pattern:

> “집중력이 떨어지거나 머리가 멍한 느낌은 흔하지만, 원인은 하나가 아닙니다. 수면, 스트레스, 약물, 기분, 대사 상태, 신경계 질환 등 다양한 요인이 관여할 수 있습니다. 이 글에서는 브레인포그라는 표현이 무엇을 뜻하는지, 현재 근거가 무엇인지, 진료가 필요한 경우를 구분해 설명합니다.”

---

## 29. Article Structure

A standard long-form article may use:

1. Title
2. Summary
3. Why the topic matters
4. Definition
5. Causes or mechanisms
6. What current evidence shows
7. What remains uncertain
8. Practical actions
9. When to seek medical care
10. Product role, only if relevant
11. Key takeaways
12. References
13. Medical and editorial review information
14. Update date

Not every article requires every section.

The structure should follow reader intent.

---

## 30. Summary Standards

The summary should:

- State the bottom line
- Avoid stronger language than the body
- Mention key uncertainty
- Avoid product promotion
- Be understandable without the full article
- Avoid unsupported numerical claims

The summary is often extracted by search engines and AI systems.

It must not oversimplify the evidence.

---

## 31. Headings and Information Hierarchy

Headings should:

- Reflect real reader questions
- Make scanning easy
- Follow a logical sequence
- Avoid repetition
- Avoid hidden claims

Use one clear H1.

H2 sections should represent major concepts.

H3 sections should clarify subtopics.

Do not use headings only for keyword repetition.

---

## 32. Paragraph and Sentence Standards

### Paragraphs

- One main idea per paragraph
- Usually two to five sentences
- Use longer paragraphs only when necessary
- Break dense scientific explanations into steps

### Sentences

- Prefer direct construction
- Limit multiple clauses
- Avoid vague pronouns
- Use active voice when clearer
- Use passive voice when the actor is unknown or not important

### Lists

Use lists when they improve scanning.

Do not turn every paragraph into bullets.

---

## 33. Readability

NervLock content should be understandable to an intelligent non-specialist unless the target audience is professional.

Readability controls include:

- Define terminology
- Use examples
- Use comparisons cautiously
- Avoid excessive abbreviations
- Limit parenthetical overload
- Use tables only when comparison benefits the reader
- Explain why a detail matters

Do not lower readability by removing necessary nuance.

---

## 34. Tables

Use tables for:

- Comparison
- Evidence grading
- Risk factors
- Symptoms versus red flags
- Product ingredient transparency
- Study differences
- Practical decision support

Tables must not:

- Oversimplify uncertainty
- Hide important footnotes
- Use unverified rankings
- Imply equivalence across incomparable studies
- Contain more detail than can be read comfortably

Every table should have a clear purpose.

---

## 35. Analogies and Metaphors

Analogies may improve understanding.

They must not distort biology.

Avoid metaphors that imply:

- The brain is “dirty”
- Toxins are “clogging” neurons
- Supplements “unlock” or “repair” the brain
- A nutrient acts like a drug without evidence
- Disease can be “flushed out”

Use analogies only when the limits are clear.

---

## 36. Stories and Anecdotes

Stories may humanize content.

They must not function as proof.

Patient stories require:

- Privacy review
- Consent
- De-identification
- Accurate representation
- No exaggerated outcome
- No hidden commercial purpose

Composite or illustrative stories must be labeled.

Do not invent a patient story and present it as real.

---

## 37. Testimonials

Testimonials must not replace evidence.

Do not use testimonials to make claims that NervLock could not lawfully make directly.

Testimonials must be:

- Genuine
- Unaltered in material meaning
- Properly disclosed
- Consented
- Contextualized
- Reviewed for regulatory and privacy risk

A disclaimer such as “individual experience” does not legalize an otherwise misleading testimonial.

---

## 38. Quotations

Use direct quotations sparingly.

Verify:

- Exact wording
- Speaker
- Source
- Context
- Permission where needed

Do not quote a source in a way that changes its certainty or meaning.

Paraphrase when it improves clarity and respects copyright.

---

## 39. References

References must follow `05_Reference_Policy.md`.

Key rules:

- Every material claim must be traceable
- Do not invent references
- Do not claim full-text review when only an abstract was accessed
- Check retractions and corrections
- Use current guidelines
- Keep citations close to claims
- Do not use references as decoration
- Do not cite a real paper for a claim it did not support

For long-form medical content, include:

- Numbered in-text citations
- Full reference list
- Review date
- Evidence cutoff date where relevant

---

## 40. Disclaimers

Disclaimers must be accurate and appropriate.

They are not substitutes for safe writing.

Possible uses include:

- General educational purpose
- No individualized diagnosis
- Need for professional consultation
- Product limitations
- Emerging evidence

A disclaimer cannot cure:

- A disease-treatment claim
- A misleading headline
- A false statistic
- An unsafe recommendation
- Hidden advertising

---

## 41. Authorship and Review Information

High-trust content should identify:

- Author
- Medical reviewer
- Editorial reviewer
- Publication date
- Last updated date
- Evidence cutoff date where relevant
- Conflicts of interest where relevant

Do not imply review that did not occur.

“Medically reviewed” requires a real documented review.

---

## 42. Conflict-of-Interest Disclosure

Disclose material relationships, including:

- Product ownership
- Advisory role
- Sponsorship
- Affiliate relationship
- Paid partnership
- Employment
- Equity
- Research funding
- Patent interest

Disclosure should be clear and proximate.

Do not hide commercial relationships in a general policy page only.

---

## 43. Correction Standards

When an error is identified:

1. Assess medical and regulatory significance
2. Correct the source content
3. Correct derivative content
4. Record the change
5. Update the date
6. Publish a correction notice when material
7. Notify relevant reviewers

Do not silently correct a material safety or factual error without preserving the record.

---

## 44. Content Update Standards

Review content when:

- A guideline changes
- Major evidence changes
- A source is corrected or retracted
- A safety warning emerges
- A law changes
- Product formulation changes
- A claim changes
- Reader questions reveal misunderstanding
- Search snippets misrepresent the content
- A medical reviewer requests revision

Evergreen content still requires periodic review.

---

## 45. Translation and Localization

Translation must preserve:

- Scientific meaning
- Evidence level
- Limitations
- Safety guidance
- Regulatory boundaries
- Tone
- Audience fit

Do not translate literally when the result is unnatural or misleading.

### Korean to English

Check:

- Regulatory terminology
- Medical terminology
- Cultural assumptions
- Public-facing claim strength
- Jurisdiction

### English to Korean

Check:

- Whether an English claim becomes stronger in Korean
- Whether medical terms need explanation
- Whether foreign regulatory status is being imported incorrectly
- Whether the Korean audience may interpret the statement as treatment advice

Translation is a new editorial version and requires review.

---

## 46. AI Drafting Rules

AI may assist with:

- Outlining
- Drafting
- Simplification
- Translation
- Summarization
- Evidence-table conversion
- Headline alternatives
- FAQ drafting
- Structural editing
- Consistency checks

AI must not:

- Invent facts
- Invent citations
- Hide uncertainty
- Strengthen claims for engagement

## 56. Quality Review Checklist

Before publication, confirm:

1. Is the audience defined?
2. Is the content purpose defined?
3. Is the primary reader intent clear?
4. Is the title accurate?
5. Does the opening avoid fear?
6. Are definitions precise?
7. Is the evidence level clear?
8. Is uncertainty visible?
9. Is mechanism separated from outcome?
10. Is association separated from causation?
11. Are statistics contextualized?
12. Are patient-important outcomes prioritized?
13. Are safety issues addressed?
14. Are red flags included where relevant?
15. Could the content delay medical care?
16. Could the content encourage self-diagnosis?
17. Could the content be interpreted as a product claim?
18. Is the commercial relationship clear?
19. Are product statements legally permitted?
20. Are references verified?
21. Are citations placed correctly?
22. Is the language calm and professional?
23. Is the content understandable?
24. Are terms used consistently?
25. Does the summary match the body?
26. Does the headline match the evidence?
27. Are CTAs appropriate?
28. Is translation accurate where applicable?
29. Has the correct review workflow occurred?
30. Has final human approval been documented?

Any unresolved material issue blocks publication.

## 57. Final Writing Principles

Good writing is not enough.

The content must also be true, safe, proportionate, useful, and defensible.

Clarity does not require oversimplification.

Authority does not require intimidation.

Uncertainty does not weaken trust.

A mechanism is not an outcome.

A citation is not decoration.

A physician title is not evidence.

A disclaimer does not repair a misleading claim.

A product should not be inserted where education is sufficient.

A headline must not be stronger than the article.

A short-form adaptation must not be stronger than the long-form source.

When evidence is strong, write clearly.

When evidence is limited, write cautiously.

When the evidence is insufficient, say so.

When the reader may need medical care, state it directly.

When a sentence exists mainly to persuade rather than inform, revise it.

NervLock writing should leave the reader better informed, less vulnerable to misinformation, and more capable of making responsible brain health decisions.
