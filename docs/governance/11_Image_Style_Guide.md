## 1. Purpose

This document defines how NervLock must plan, create, select, review, approve, publish, reuse, and archive visual assets.

It governs:

- Brand imagery
- Website visuals
- Blog images
- Instagram posts
- Instagram Stories
- Reels covers
- Facebook graphics
- Threads graphics
- YouTube thumbnails
- YouTube Shorts covers
- Infographics
- Medical illustrations
- Scientific diagrams
- Data visualizations
- Product images
- Packaging mockups
- Photography
- AI-generated images
- AI-assisted image editing
- Icons
- Charts
- Tables
- Educational carousels
- Patient-education visuals
- Professional education visuals
- Press images
- Newsletter graphics
- Visual adaptation across channels

The goal is not to make NervLock merely look attractive.

The goal is to make complex brain health information:

- Easier to understand
- Safer to interpret
- Visually consistent
- Medically responsible
- Regulatorily defensible
- Accessible
- Distinctive
- Trustworthy

## 2. Governing Visual Principle

NervLock follows one central visual rule:

> Every visual must clarify the message without exaggerating the evidence.

A visual must not:

- Create a stronger claim than the text
- Imply diagnosis
- Imply treatment
- Imply disease reversal
- Convert a mechanism into a proven outcome
- Use fear to increase engagement
- Use physician imagery to pressure purchase
- Make a product look like medicine when it is not
- Hide limitations through design
- Use aesthetic polish as a substitute for scientific accuracy

The overall impression of the image, headline, caption, iconography, color, composition, and CTA must be reviewed together.

## 3. Strategic Role of Visuals

Visual assets should support five functions.

### 3.1 Comprehension

Help readers understand:

- Anatomy
- Mechanisms
- Evidence strength
- Risk
- Symptoms
- Care pathways
- Lifestyle principles
- Product rationale
- Safety

### 3.2 Recognition

Create consistent NervLock brand recognition across:

- Website
- Blog
- Instagram
- Facebook
- Threads
- YouTube
- Products
- Educational materials

### 3.3 Trust

Signal:

- Professional restraint
- Scientific seriousness
- Medical responsibility
- Transparency
- Calm authority

### 3.4 Navigation

Help the user identify:

- Content type
- Topic
- Evidence level
- Red flags
- Practical next steps
- Product versus education

### 3.5 Repurposing

Allow a strong core visual system to be adapted across channels without changing the underlying medical meaning.

## 4. Visual Positioning

NervLock visuals should communicate:

**A neurologist-led, evidence-based brain health platform that explains science clearly and responsibly.**

The visual system should not primarily resemble:

- A supplement advertisement
- A beauty brand
- A luxury cosmetic brand
- A pharmaceutical campaign
- A fear-based disease campaign
- A generic wellness influencer account
- A children’s cartoon channel
- A biohacking gadget brand
- A hospital brochure
- A low-cost content farm

The desired visual response is:

> “This is clear, credible, and thoughtfully explained.”

not:

> “This is dramatic, urgent, and trying to sell me something.”

## 6. Visual Hierarchy

Every public visual should prioritize information in this order:

1. Topic
2. Core educational message
3. Evidence or context
4. Practical meaning
5. Safety or red flags
6. Brand identity
7. CTA
8. Product, only when relevant

The brand logo must not compete with the educational message.

A purchase CTA must not dominate an educational image.

A red-flag message must not be visually minimized.

## 8. Visual Risk Classification

### Level 0 — Administrative

Examples:

- Internal workflow diagram
- File map
- Project process graphic

### Level 1 — Low-Risk Education

Examples:

- Basic brain anatomy
- Sleep hygiene
- Exercise education

### Level 2 — Medical Education

Examples:

- Symptom infographic
- Disease overview
- Clinical pathway
- Mechanism diagram

### Level 3 — High-Risk Commercial or Clinical Visual

Examples:

- Product efficacy graphic
- Before-and-after
- Physician endorsement
- Patient case
- Treatment comparison
- Disease-related product image
- Diagnostic scan

- Product shown repairing neurons
- Fake institutional endorsement

## 9. Color Strategy

The NervLock color system should communicate:

- Scientific credibility
- Calmness
- Brain health
- Clarity
- Long-term trust

The final approved brand palette should be documented separately with exact color values.

Until a formal palette is approved:

- Do not invent permanent brand hex values
- Do not allow each designer or AI tool to create a new palette
- Use a restrained, low-noise visual system
- Prefer a neutral background with one controlled primary accent and one secondary accent
- Reserve high-saturation colors for small functional cues
- Maintain sufficient contrast
- Avoid visual associations with emergency, danger, or pharmaceutical treatment unless context requires them

### Preferred Color Behavior

- Calm cool or neutral base
- Moderate contrast
- Limited number of colors
- Consistent accent use
- Clear semantic use of red, amber, green, and blue

### Avoid

- Neon gradients
- Rainbow palettes
- Heavy purple-blue “AI” clichés
- Excessive red
- Black-and-red fear aesthetics
- Pastel combinations that reduce legibility
- Colors selected only because they are trending

## 10. Semantic Color Use

Use color consistently.

### Red

Use only for:

- Urgent danger
- Contraindication
- Red flag
- Critical error
- Prohibited claim

Do not use red to increase product urgency.

### Amber or Orange

Use for:

- Caution
- Emerging evidence
- Qualification
- Moderate risk
- Needs review

### Green

Use for:

- Generally supported action
- Approved status
- Normal range
- Low risk

Green must not imply guaranteed safety or efficacy.

### Blue or Neutral Accent

Use for:

- Education
- Explanation
- Definitions
- Navigation
- Evidence summaries

### Gray

Use for:

- Secondary text
- Background structure
- Limitations
- Neutral comparison

Do not use low-contrast gray for important safety information.

## 11. Typography

Typography must support:

- Korean readability
- English readability
- Medical terminology
- Mobile legibility
- Long-form education
- Strong hierarchy

### General Rules

- Use one primary type family or a tightly controlled pair
- Preserve clear H1, H2, H3, body, caption, and label hierarchy
- Use bold for emphasis, not decoration
- Avoid long all-caps English text
- Avoid decorative display fonts for medical content
- Avoid overly condensed fonts
- Avoid very light weights on light backgrounds
- Avoid mixing multiple type styles in one asset

### Korean Text

Korean should have:

- Adequate line height
- Generous letter spacing only when needed
- Clear word wrapping
- No excessive character compression
- No tiny footnotes that become unreadable on mobile

### English Text

English should preserve:

- Correct capitalization
- Clear line breaks
- Natural phrasing
- No forced line wrapping that changes meaning

---

## 12. Text Density

NervLock visuals should prioritize comprehension over information volume.

### General Rule

One visual should communicate one primary message.

### Recommended

- One main title
- One supporting message
- One to three key points
- One CTA area
- One brand mark

### Avoid

- Paragraphs inside graphics
- Dense tables in social images
- More than four competing message blocks
- Small legal text placed only to fit more claims
- Repetition of the caption inside the image
- Overloaded icons
- Excessive footnotes

When information is complex, create a multi-card series instead of shrinking text.

## 13. Safe-Zone Standard for 9:16 Content

For Instagram Stories, Reels covers, YouTube Shorts covers, and other vertical formats, NervLock uses a 9:16 working canvas.

### Primary Working Canvas

- 1080 × 1920 pixels

### Safe-Zone Principle

All important content must remain within the central safe area.

Use approximately:

- 15% left margin
- 15% right margin
- At least 120 pixels top margin
- At least 150 pixels bottom margin

The central 70% of the canvas should contain:

- Title
- Main visual
- Key message
- CTA
- Logo

No essential text, icon, face, product, or medical label should touch the outer edges.

### Additional Rules

- Keep the title below the top interface area
- Keep CTA above the bottom interface area
- Keep subtitles away from right-side engagement controls
- Keep logos away from corners
- Test with actual platform cropping before publication

## 15. Instagram Story Standards

Stories should:

- Use 9:16
- Keep one core message per screen
- Use large text
- Use strong contrast
- Use one clear interaction element
- Maintain safe margins
- Preserve NervLock identity
- Avoid long explanatory paragraphs

### Recommended Story Sequence

1. Hook or question
2. Explanation
3. Key takeaway
4. Next action or interaction

### Story Interaction

Use sparingly:

- Poll
- Question box
- Quiz
- Slider
- Link sticker

Do not combine several interaction elements on one screen.

---

## 16. Instagram Carousel Standards

A carousel should have a defined narrative.

### Recommended Structure

1. Cover
2. Definition or problem
3. Explanation
4. Evidence
5. Practical meaning
6. Red flags or limitations
7. Key takeaway
8. CTA

The number of cards should follow the topic.

Do not force every carousel to use the same number of cards.

### Cover Card

The cover should contain:

- Short title
- One visual concept
- Optional subtitle
- Small brand identifier

The cover must not contain the entire article.

---

## 17. CTA Standard for Social Visuals

Use no more than:

- One Save CTA
- One Follow CTA

Preferred footer/action area:

- “🔖 저장”
- “➕ 팔로우”

Do not repeat save prompts in multiple places.

Do not combine:

- Save
- Share
- Comment
- Follow
- Buy
- DM
- Link click

on the same visual.

The CTA must match the content purpose.

---

## 18. YouTube Thumbnail Standards

YouTube thumbnails should:

- Communicate one idea
- Use a short title
- Use strong visual separation
- Preserve professional tone
- Avoid misleading drama
- Remain understandable at small size

### Avoid

- Shocked facial expressions
- Red arrows and circles without informational value
- Misleading scans
- “You won’t believe”
- “Doctors hide this”
- Disease fear
- Product guarantees
- Excessive text

The thumbnail may simplify the topic, but it must not distort the video’s conclusion.

---

## 19. Reels and Shorts Cover Standards

Covers should:

- Use 9:16
- Keep title in the central safe zone
- Use the same series template where appropriate
- Preserve readable profile-grid crop
- Avoid placing critical text near the bottom
- Avoid relying on automatic caption placement
- Use one visual focus

The cover should communicate the topic, not the entire lesson.

## 22. Representation and Inclusion

Visuals should avoid stereotypes.

Do not assume:

- Older age equals dementia
- Tremor equals Parkinson’s disease
- Memory concern equals Alzheimer’s disease
- Pain equals visible suffering
- Disability equals dependency
- Wellness equals youth and thinness

Use diverse and respectful representation.

Do not use identifiable patient images without valid rights, consent, privacy review, and regulatory review.

## 23. Medical Illustration Standard

Medical illustrations must identify whether they are:

- Anatomical
- Diagnostic
- Mechanistic
- Conceptual
- Illustrative
- AI-generated

### Anatomical

Must be structurally reasonable and labeled appropriately.

### Diagnostic

Must not be fabricated or presented as a real patient result unless authentic, de-identified, permitted, and clinically reviewed.

### Mechanistic

Must not imply that pathway activation proves clinical benefit.

### Conceptual

Must be labeled or clearly designed so the viewer does not mistake it for literal anatomy or diagnosis.

### AI-Generated

Must be reviewed carefully for:

- Anatomical errors
- Impossible structures
- Incorrect lesion location
- False laterality
- Extra or missing body parts
- Incorrect medical devices
- Implausible physiology

---

## 24. Brain Illustration Standard

Brain visuals should avoid the common error of implying that a product directly repairs or protects the brain.

Use brain images to explain:

- Anatomy
- Networks
- Function
- Symptoms
- Lifestyle context
- Evidence concepts

Do not show:

- Neurons visibly regrowing after a supplement
- Plaque disappearing because of a product
- A dark brain becoming bright after use
- Brain aging being reversed
- A capsule entering the brain as proof of efficacy
- A product “locking” the brain in a literal medical sense

The brand name NervLock must not be visualized as a literal guarantee of neural protection.

---

## 25. Mechanism Diagram Standard

Every mechanism diagram should distinguish:

- Established pathway
- Proposed pathway
- Hypothesis
- Unknown link

Use labels such as:

- Established
- Supported
- Emerging
- Hypothesized
- Unknown

Arrows must represent a real relationship.

Avoid decorative arrows that imply causality.

Do not use a mechanistic chain to support a clinical claim unless human outcome evidence exists.

---

## 26. Evidence Visualization Standard

Visual evidence summaries should show:

- Evidence type
- Certainty
- Population
- Outcome
- Limitations
- Applicability

### Preferred Formats

- Evidence ladder
- Evidence matrix
- Study-design comparison
- Benefit-harm balance
- What we know / what we do not know
- Timeline of evidence
- Population applicability map

### Avoid

- Star ratings without defined criteria
- “Science score” without methodology
- Percentage confidence invented by the designer
- Decorative citation counts
- Journal logos as proof of truth

---

## 27. Data Visualization Standard

Charts must:

- Use real data
- Identify source
- Identify units
- Identify denominator
- Identify time period
- Identify population
- Use an appropriate scale
- Avoid distorted axes
- Avoid decorative 3D effects
- Avoid selective cropping
- Preserve uncertainty where relevant

### For Medical Data

Show when possible:

- Absolute values
- Confidence intervals
- Baseline risk
- Sample size
- Study limitations

Do not use relative effects alone when they materially exaggerate impact.

---

## 28. Chart Color Rules

Charts should:

- Use a limited palette
- Remain understandable in grayscale where feasible
- Use direct labels where possible
- Avoid red-green dependence
- Maintain contrast
- Avoid using brand color as proof of superiority

Color should encode information consistently.

Do not use more categories than the chart can support clearly.

---

## 29. Tables

Tables should be used when comparison improves understanding.

Use tables for:

- Symptoms and red flags
- Evidence comparison
- Ingredient rationale
- Dose and safety
- Study differences
- Practical decision support

Tables must:

- Have clear headers
- Avoid tiny text
- Work on mobile
- Avoid too many columns
- Preserve medical meaning
- Identify limitations

For social media, split complex tables into multiple cards.

---

## 30. Icons

Icons should be:

- Simple
- Consistent
- Recognizable
- Secondary to the message
- Visually coherent across a series

Avoid:

- Mixing line icons, 3D icons, emoji, and realistic illustrations in one asset
- Using a shield icon to imply guaranteed protection
- Using a check mark to imply clinical proof
- Using a hospital cross to imply medical treatment
- Using a brain-plus symbol to imply cognitive enhancement

Icons must not create unauthorized claims.

---

## 31. Illustration Style Consistency

A visual series must use one illustration system.

Do not mix:

- Photorealistic organs
- Cartoon organs
- Pixar-like characters
- Flat vectors
- Clinical scans
- 3D renders

within the same series unless the contrast has a deliberate educational purpose.

For a multi-scene video or carousel:

- Character style must remain consistent
- Organ style must remain consistent
- Lighting must remain consistent
- Camera perspective must remain coherent
- Color system must remain coherent
- Visual complexity must remain similar

Consistency is more important than novelty.

---

## 32. 3D Illustration Standard

3D illustration may be used for:

- Brain anatomy
- Conceptual pathways
- Educational organ systems
- Lifestyle scenes

It should be:

- Clean
- Softly lit
- Anatomically plausible
- Not grotesque
- Not excessively glossy
- Not toy-like unless clearly conceptual

Avoid inconsistent rendering quality across cards or scenes.

Do not use 3D effects merely to make weak evidence appear more scientific.

---

## 33. Stylized Character Standard

Stylized characters may be used for:

- General lifestyle education
- Stress
- Sleep
- Exercise
- Everyday symptoms
- Caregiver support

They should not be used for:

- Serious diagnosis
- Medical emergency
- Product efficacy
- Patient testimonials
- Complex neurological pathology

When used, the character design must remain:

- Respectful
- Adult-appropriate
- Non-infantilizing
- Consistent across the series

## 34. AI-Generated Image Policy

AI-generated images are permitted as working tools and public assets only after review.

### AI May Be Used For

- Concept development
- Background generation
- Educational illustration
- Stylized anatomy
- Social media visuals
- Thumbnail concepts
- Non-identifiable lifestyle scenes
- Product mockups before photography

### AI Must Not Be Used To

- Fabricate real patient outcomes
- Create fake medical scans
- Create fake testimonials
- Imply real physician endorsement without consent
- Reproduce a living artist’s distinctive style as a direct imitation
- Replicate copyrighted artwork too closely
- Generate false product packaging
- Show unauthorized logos
- Create misleading before-and-after results

---

## 35. AI Image Review

Every AI-generated public visual must be checked for:

- Anatomical accuracy
- Clinical plausibility
- Number of fingers and limbs
- Correct laterality
- Correct text
- Correct product label
- Correct logo
- Correct proportions
- Correct device placement
- Correct ethnic and age representation
- Privacy risk
- Copyright similarity
- Regulatory implication
- Brand consistency

AI-generated text inside an image must be manually verified.

Do not trust generated Korean text without review.

---

## 36. Style-Transfer and Copyright Rules

Do not request or publish a close imitation of:

- A living artist
- A recognizable commercial franchise
- A specific copyrighted illustration
- A competitor’s campaign
- A medical publisher’s proprietary artwork

Use broad style descriptors such as:

- Clean editorial 3D
- Soft medical illustration
- Modern scientific infographic
- Minimal clinical line art
- Warm professional photography

When adapting a reference image:

- Preserve only the concept
- Change composition
- Change illustration language
- Change visual hierarchy
- Change layout
- Change iconography
- Avoid tracing
- Avoid near-copying

---

## 37. Source and Rights Management

Every visual asset should have a rights record.

Record:

- Asset ID
- Creator
- Source
- License
- Purchase date
- Usage scope
- Model release
- Property release
- Attribution requirement
- Edit permission
- Expiration
- AI-generated status
- Original prompt or project file

Do not publish an image merely because it was found online.

---

## 38. Patient and Clinical Image Policy

Patient or clinical images require heightened controls.

Before use, confirm:

- Lawful basis
- Valid consent
- De-identification
- Usage scope
- Platform
- Duration
- Right to withdraw where applicable
- Whether the image is necessary
- Whether the image could be stigmatizing
- Whether medical advertising review applies

Do not rely only on facial blurring.

Clinical context may still identify a patient.

---

## 39. Before-and-After Visuals

Before-and-after images are presumptively high risk.

They may not be used without:

- Scientific review
- Medical review
- Regulatory review
- Consent review
- Image-integrity review
- Final human approval

Do not use:

- Different lighting
- Different posture
- Different angle
- Different makeup
- Different image quality
- Cropping that exaggerates change
- Retouching
- AI enhancement that alters outcome
- Product attribution when causality is uncertain

For most NervLock public education and product content, before-and-after imagery should be avoided.

---

## 40. Physician Imagery

Physician imagery may establish authorship and accountability.

It must not function as coercive endorsement.

### Safer Uses

- Author profile
- Editorial introduction
- Educational video
- About page
- Evidence explanation

### High-Risk Uses

- Physician holding product
- White coat next to efficacy claim
- Product plus hospital-like background
- “Doctor recommended” without defined basis
- Physician image beside disease-treatment language

The physician’s presence cannot replace evidence or legal permission.

---

## 41. Product Photography

Product photography must show the actual product accurately.

Required:

- Correct packaging
- Correct label
- Correct color
- Correct serving form
- Correct count or size
- No false scale
- No hidden required warnings
- No visual implication of medicine

### Product Lifestyle Scenes

May show:

- Daily routine
- Food context
- Desk
- Travel
- General wellness

Must not show:

- Disease recovery
- Hospital discharge
- Medication replacement
- Cognitive transformation
- Performance guarantee

---

## 42. Product Mockups

Product mockups may be used internally before final photography.

Public use requires confirmation that:

- Packaging matches the final product
- Label text is approved
- Claims are approved
- Color and materials are accurate
- Product size is not misleading

Label the asset internally as:

- Concept
- Draft mockup
- Approved mockup
- Final product photography

Do not publish a concept mockup as the final product.

---

## 43. Packaging Visual Rules

Packaging should communicate:

- Product identity
- Category
- Transparency
- Safety
- Calm confidence

It should not imitate:

- Prescription medicine
- Emergency medicine
- Hospital devices
- Diagnostic kits

unless the product is legally classified as such.

Avoid:

- Brain scan imagery
- Medical cross symbols
- “Rx-like” design
- Capsule-to-brain pathway claims
- Disease names
- Unapproved functional claims

---

## 44. Medical Red-Flag Visuals

Red-flag visuals must be:

- Easy to notice
- Specific
- Proportionate
- Non-sensational
- Action-oriented

Use:

- Clear title
- Limited number of red flags
- Direct next step
- Emergency versus routine distinction

Avoid:

- Horror imagery
- Panic language
- Graphic pathology
- Excessive red
- Ambiguous urgency

---

## 45. Evidence-Level Badge System

A badge system may be used only when definitions are fixed.

Suggested internal categories:

- Established
- Supported but Qualified
- Emerging
- Hypothesis
- Insufficient Evidence

Public use requires:

- Visible legend
- Consistent criteria
- No decorative use
- No conversion into a numeric score

The badge must reflect the reviewed evidence, not marketing preference.

---

## 46. Infographic Structure

A strong NervLock infographic should contain:

1. Topic title
2. One-sentence orientation
3. Main explanation
4. Practical meaning
5. Limitation or uncertainty
6. Red flag when relevant
7. Source or reference cue
8. Brand and CTA

Not every infographic needs all elements, but the medical meaning must remain complete.

---

## 47. Infographic Writing

Image text should:

- Use short sentences
- Use plain language
- Preserve medical accuracy
- Avoid unsupported certainty
- Avoid unqualified statistics
- Avoid unexplained abbreviations
- Avoid unnecessary jargon

The caption may carry deeper nuance.

The image must remain accurate even when seen without the caption.

---

## 48. Caption and Image Relationship

The image and caption must not contradict each other.

The caption may:

- Expand evidence
- Add nuance
- Add references
- Add limitations
- Add red flags

The caption must not be used to repair a misleading headline or visual.

The image must stand on its own without creating a false claim.

---

## 49. Source Display in Visuals

For scientific or medical visuals, use an appropriate source cue.

Options include:

- Short source line
- Reference number
- “근거 요약”
- “가이드라인 기반”
- Link in caption or article

Do not place unreadably small citations.

For social content:

- Use concise source labels on the card
- Provide full references in caption, linked article, or source slide

Do not display journal logos without permission.

---

## 50. Alt Text

Alt text should describe:

- What the image shows
- The relevant educational meaning
- Any text that is not otherwise available
- Important relationships

Alt text should not:

- Repeat decorative details
- Add claims not shown
- Use keyword stuffing
- Reveal private information
- Replace a proper caption when a caption is needed

For charts, provide a concise interpretation and access to the underlying values where practical.

---

## 51. Accessibility in Visual Design

All visuals should consider:

- Color contrast
- Font size
- Line spacing
- Reading order
- Color-blind accessibility
- Alternative text
- Captions
- Transcripts
- Motion sensitivity
- Screen-reader compatibility
- Mobile zoom

Do not communicate critical information through color alone.

Do not use flashing or rapid motion.

---

## 52. Motion and Animation

Animation may be used to explain:

- Sequence
- Process
- Movement
- Comparison
- Cause-and-effect hypothesis

Animation must not:

- Imply certainty beyond evidence
- Show impossible anatomy
- Show product effects not demonstrated
- Move so quickly that warnings cannot be read
- Use dramatic sound to create fear

Use motion sparingly.

---

## 53. Video Scene Consistency

For animated or AI-generated videos:

- Use one visual style
- Use one character system
- Use one organ-rendering system
- Use consistent lighting
- Use consistent camera language
- Use consistent color grading
- Use consistent text placement
- Use consistent caption style

Do not accept a sequence in which each scene appears generated by a different studio unless intentionally designed and approved.

---

## 54. Subtitle and Caption Design

Subtitles should be:

- Large enough
- High contrast
- Within the safe zone
- Synchronized
- Free from transcription errors
- Limited to readable line length

For Korean:

- Avoid breaking words unnaturally
- Avoid covering the speaker’s face
- Avoid placing subtitles over key anatomical labels

Auto-generated subtitles must be reviewed before publication.

### Example Prompt Structure

> Create a 9:16 educational brain health illustration for Instagram Story. Use a calm, modern, medically responsible editorial 3D style. Keep all important content inside the central 70% safe zone, with generous top and bottom margins. Show an anatomically plausible brain as a conceptual educational visual, not a diagnostic image. - Move the main brain illustration upward
- Increase side margins
- Reduce the number of icons

- Change only illustration style
- Maintain the safe zone
- Remove the product from the educational visual

- “More premium”
- “More viral”

A NervLock visual is publication-ready only when it is:

- Clear
- Legible
- Consistent
- Respectful
- Accessible
- Rights-cleared
- Medically reviewed when needed
- Regulatorily reviewed when needed
- Brand-consistent
- Technically correct
- Human-approved

Visual polish is not approval.

AI generation is not authorship.

A doctor image is not evidence.

A brain image is not proof.

A disclaimer is not a repair tool.

A viral format is not a strategic reason.

A product should not become the visual center of disease education.

## 88. Final Visual Principle

Every NervLock visual should answer:

1. What should the viewer understand?
2. What should the viewer not infer?
3. How strong is the evidence?
4. What action is reasonable?
5. Is any medical evaluation needed?
6. Is the visual educational or commercial?
7. Does the image strengthen long-term trust?

When the visual cannot answer these questions clearly, revise it before publication.

NervLock should use visual communication to make science clearer, not to make marketing stronger.
