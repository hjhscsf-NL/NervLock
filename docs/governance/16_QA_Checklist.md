## 2. Governing QA Principle

NervLock follows one central quality rule:

> Nothing is complete merely because it has been written, designed, coded, recorded, uploaded, or approved by an AI system.

A NervLock asset is complete only when:

- The objective is clear
- The governing documents were applied
- The risk was classified
- The evidence was verified
- The medical meaning is accurate
- The regulatory context is acceptable
- References are traceable
- The brand position is preserved
- The user experience is safe and clear
- The correct human authority approved the final version
- The version and approval record are preserved

Fluency is not quality assurance.

Visual polish is not quality assurance.

Search performance is not quality assurance.

A physician title is not quality assurance.

A disclaimer is not quality assurance.

AI confidence is not quality assurance.

## 3. Quality Hierarchy

When quality objectives conflict, apply this order:

1. Applicable law
2. Immediate public and patient safety
3. Medical accuracy
4. Regulatory compliance
5. Reference integrity
6. Privacy and rights
7. Brand integrity
8. Reader comprehension
9. Accessibility
10. Technical correctness
11. Search discoverability
12. Engagement
13. Conversion
14. Publication speed

No lower-priority objective may override a higher-priority requirement.

Examples:

- SEO must not strengthen a medical claim.
- Conversion must not obscure product limitations.
- Visual simplicity must not remove a red flag.
- Publication speed must not bypass reference verification.
- Technical convenience must not alter approved wording.

## 5. QA Status Labels

Use the following controlled statuses.

### Not Started

No substantive review has begun.

### In Development

Work is being created.

### Internal Draft

Not ready for public-facing review.

### Evidence Review

Scientific evidence is being assessed.

### Medical Review

Clinical accuracy and safety are being assessed.

### Regulatory Review

Claims and commercial context are being assessed.

### Reference Audit

Sources and claim mapping are being verified.

### Brand Review

Positioning, tone, and trust are being assessed.

### Technical Review

Implementation, accessibility, SEO, performance, or platform behavior is being assessed.

### Revision Required

Material changes are required.

### Re-Review Required

A revised version must return to one or more reviewers.

### Approved With Conditions

Approval is limited to documented conditions.

### Approved for Publication

Authorized human approval has been recorded for the exact version and channel.

### Published

The approved version is publicly accessible.

### Update Required

A live asset requires review or revision.

### Withdrawn

Use has been stopped.

### Archived

The asset is retained for records but is not active.

## 6. Review Outcome Labels

Every review should conclude with one of the following:

- **Pass**
- **Pass With Minor Corrections**
- **Pass With Conditions**
- **Revision Required**
- **Specialist Review Required**
- **Publication Blocked**
- **Use Not Recommended**
- **Prohibited**

Do not use vague outcomes such as:

- Looks good
- Probably fine
- Ready enough
- Minor issue
- Should be okay

without documenting the exact issue and decision.

## 7. Issue Severity Classification

### Critical

A problem that may cause serious harm, unlawful publication, major deception, privacy breach, or material reputational damage.

Examples:

- Disease-treatment claim for a supplement
- Advice to stop medication
- Fabricated reference
- Unconsented patient disclosure
- Dangerous medical error
- Incorrect product formula
- Undisclosed serious adverse-event risk
- Hacked or fraudulent page

Action:

- Block publication
- Stop distribution
- Escalate immediately
- Preserve evidence
- Correct or withdraw

### Major

A material problem that changes meaning, risk, or compliance.

Examples:

- Overstated evidence
- Missing red flags
- Wrong dose
- Wrong population
- Misleading product visual
- Unverified numerical claim
- Missing commercial disclosure
- Broken canonical after migration

Action:

- Block publication until corrected
- Require re-review

### Moderate

A problem that may impair understanding, trust, accessibility, or operational quality.

Examples:

- Ambiguous wording
- Weak internal links
- Incomplete alt text
- Missing update date
- Inconsistent terminology
- Poor mobile layout

Action:

- Correct before publication when practical
- Document accepted residual issue if publication proceeds

### Minor

A low-impact problem that does not materially change meaning or safety.

Examples:

- Typographic error
- Spacing inconsistency
- Non-material formatting issue

Action:

- Correct before or promptly after publication
- No full re-review unless meaning changes

## 8. Automatic Publication Blockers

Publication must stop when any of the following is unresolved:

- Product or service classification is unknown
- A material medical claim lacks verified support
- A reference may be fabricated
- A material source is retracted
- The wording exceeds the evidence
- The wording exceeds regulatory permission
- Disease-treatment or prevention is implied for a food or supplement
- A red flag is omitted where omission could cause harm
- Individualized treatment is provided without an appropriate clinical relationship
- Medication discontinuation or replacement is advised unsafely
- Patient privacy or consent is unresolved
- Commercial relationship is undisclosed
- Product formula, dose, label, or pack image is inaccurate
- Adverse-event or safety information is incomplete
- Final human approval is absent
- The implemented version differs materially from the approved version
- The wrong file or language version is scheduled
- A paid campaign lacks separate advertising review
- Copyright or licensing is unresolved
- A critical accessibility barrier prevents access to essential safety information

## 9. Risk Classification

### Level 0 — Administrative

Examples:

- Internal checklist
- File naming
- Task assignment

Minimum review:

- Accuracy
- Version control

### Level 1 — Low-Risk Education

Examples:

- Basic anatomy
- General exercise
- General sleep hygiene

Minimum review:

- Editorial
- Brand
- Basic factual verification

### Level 2 — Medical Education

Examples:

- Symptom education
- Disease overview
- Guideline summary
- Research explanation
- Supplement evidence

Minimum review:

- Scientific
- Medical safety
- Reference
- Brand
- Channel-specific QA

### Level 3 — High-Risk Clinical or Commercial

Examples:

- Product page
- Advertisement
- Dose recommendation
- Physician product endorsement
- Testimonial
- Before-and-after
- Paid campaign
- Product-linked disease content

Minimum review:

- Scientific
- Medical safety
- Regulatory
- Reference audit
- Brand
- Technical or channel review
- Final human approval

### Level 4 — Prohibited

Examples:

- Fabricated evidence
- Guaranteed disease prevention
- Product treatment claim
- Dangerous medical instruction
- Fake patient result
- False physician endorsement

Action:

- Do not publish
- Record the reason
- Provide a compliant alternative when feasible

## 11. Final Approval Boundary

Only an authorized human reviewer may assign:

`Approved for Publication`

AI systems may:

- Review
- Flag
- Recommend
- Compare
- Draft
- Generate checklists
- Identify unresolved risks

AI systems may not:

- Grant final medical approval
- Grant final regulatory approval
- Grant final product approval
- Grant final publication approval
- Approve their own output
- Mark a draft as final without human authority

Approval applies only to:

- Exact version
- Exact language
- Exact channel
- Exact product
- Exact claim
- Exact visual
- Exact commercial context

Material changes invalidate the approval.

## 94. Rapid Pre-Publication Gate

## 95. QA Scorecard

A numerical score may support prioritization but may not override blockers.

Suggested internal domains:

| Domain | Maximum |
|---|---:|
| Strategic fit | 10 |
| Scientific accuracy | 20 |
| Medical safety | 20 |
| Regulatory compliance | 20 |
| Reference integrity | 10 |
| Brand consistency | 5 |
| Comprehension and accessibility | 5 |
| Technical and channel quality | 5 |
| Updateability and traceability | 5 |
| **Total** | **100** |

Suggested internal interpretation:

- 95–100: Strong, subject to no blockers and final approval
- 90–94: Usable after minor corrections
- 80–89: Material revision required
- Below 80: Not ready
- Any critical blocker: Publication blocked regardless of score

A high score cannot compensate for a critical issue.

## 100. Reviewer Independence

For high-risk content:

- The drafter should not be the only reviewer.
- The product owner should not be the only regulatory reviewer.
- The reference collector should not be the only reference auditor.
- The implementation partner should not approve its own changes.

- Commercial urgency should not control scientific conclusions.

Independence may be proportionate to team size, but conflict must be acknowledged and documented.

## 105. Final QA Principles

Quality assurance begins before drafting.

It does not begin after publication.

Every public claim is also:

- A scientific statement
- A medical communication
- A regulatory representation
- A brand decision
- A trust decision

The title is part of the claim.

The thumbnail is part of the claim.

The image is part of the claim.

The pinned comment is part of the claim.

The product link is part of the claim.

The checkout page is part of the claim.

A derivative asset is part of the original content system.

A correction is incomplete until material derivatives are corrected.

A high score cannot override a critical blocker.

A deadline cannot override safety.

An AI system cannot approve itself.

The final question before publication is not:

> “Is this likely to perform?”

It is:

> “Is this accurate, safe, lawful, transparent, useful, and worthy of long-term trust?”

Only after that question is answered should performance and conversion be considered.
