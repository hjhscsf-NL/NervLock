## 1. Purpose

This roadmap defines the current product-development direction for NervLock.

It converts the product-governance principles in `06_Product_Guide.md` into an active portfolio plan.

It records:

- Current product concepts
- Strategic rationale
- Product priority
- Development stage
- Open questions
- Evidence requirements
- Safety requirements
- Regulatory dependencies
- Quality requirements
- Commercial assumptions
- Decision gates
- Milestones
- Owners
- Risks
- Stop criteria
- Launch sequencing
- Post-launch review requirements

This roadmap is not:

- A final formulation
- A product label
- A permitted-claims document
- A manufacturing order
- A launch authorization
- A public marketing plan
- A substitute for current regulatory review

Every product remains subject to scientific, safety, quality, regulatory, commercial, and final human approval.

---

## 2. Governing Product Roadmap Principle

NervLock follows one central roadmap rule:

> Product development must proceed only when a clearly defined need, relevant human evidence, acceptable safety, controllable quality, regulatory feasibility, and long-term brand value converge.

A product must not advance because:

- The ingredient is popular
- A competitor is selling it
- The market appears large
- A supplier offers a ready-made formula
- A mechanism sounds persuasive
- A high dose appears impressive
- A physician title may improve conversion
- A launch deadline has already been announced

Commercial momentum must not override a failed product gate.

---

## 3. Strategic Role of Products

Products are one component of the NervLock ecosystem.

They must support, not replace:

- Brain health education
- Scientific interpretation
- Medical responsibility
- Sustainable lifestyle practices
- Public health literacy
- Long-term trust

The required strategic sequence remains:

**Brain Health**  
→ **Education**  
→ **Science**  
→ **Trust**  
→ **Products**

The first product must establish the standard for every later product.

It should therefore prioritize:

- Scientific defensibility
- Formula transparency
- Conservative positioning
- Reliable quality
- Manageable safety
- Operational simplicity
- Clear consumer need
- Educational coherence

The first product should not attempt to solve every brain health concern.

## 4. Roadmap Objectives

### 4.1 Primary Objective

Develop one first NervLock product that can be explained honestly, manufactured reliably, and positioned without unsupported disease claims.

### 4.2 Secondary Objectives

- Establish a repeatable product-development system
- Build product evidence dossiers
- Establish supplier and manufacturer qualification standards
- Establish quality specifications
- Build complaint, adverse-event, and recall systems
- Create transparent product-science pages
- Connect product education to the broader brain health platform
- Create a disciplined sequence for later products
- Avoid premature portfolio expansion

### 4.3 Non-Objectives

The initial roadmap does not aim to:

- Launch many SKUs quickly
- Enter every brain health category
- Compete primarily on ingredient count
- Compete primarily on highest dose
- Use disease fear to create demand
- Depend on unsupported proprietary synergy
- Build a product catalog before the educational platform is mature

## 6. Current Strategic Assessment

### 6.1 Brain Oil Softgel

Strategic strengths:

- Familiar consumer category
- Relatively clear ingredient identity
- Broad educational relevance
- Established manufacturing ecosystem
- Softgel format is expected by consumers
- Easier to explain as a focused single-category product
- May provide a lower-complexity operational pilot

Strategic weaknesses:

- Highly competitive market
- Difficult differentiation
- Risk of becoming a commodity product
- Quality differences are not always visible to consumers
- Oxidation, source, purification, and stability must be managed rigorously
- Product may not communicate a sufficiently distinctive NervLock brain-health identity
- Disease-prevention or memory claims may be easily overstated in marketing

### 6.2 Brain Core Capsule

Strategic strengths:

- Stronger alignment with a brain-specific flagship identity
- More differentiated educational narrative
- Clear opportunity to explain ingredient identity, dose, and evidence
- Potentially better fit with the NervLock platform positioning
- May create a stronger foundation for a future cognitive-aging product system

Strategic weaknesses:

- Higher formulation complexity
- Potential capsule burden
- Potential cost pressure
- Source, purity, identity, and stability require careful control
- Combination formula may blur the primary product rationale
- Adding several vitamins may make the formula appear generic
- Evidence for individual ingredients must not be represented as finished-product evidence
- Product communication may drift toward unsupported cognitive-performance claims

### 6.3 Current Portfolio Conclusion

Current internal position:

- **Brain Core Capsule is the stronger brand-fit candidate.**
- **Brain Oil Softgel is the lower execution-risk candidate.**
- **No final first-product selection has been made.**
- **Launching both products simultaneously is not the preferred default.**

The recommended decision process is:

1. Complete feasibility and evidence dossiers for both concepts.
2. Compare cost, capsule burden, quality control, regulatory pathway, supplier reliability, and communication territory.
3. Select one flagship product.
4. Develop the second product only after the first product passes operational and market validation.

## 7. Recommended Launch Sequence

### Preferred Strategic Sequence

**Option 1 — Brain Core First**

Proceed when all of the following are satisfied:

- 300 mg daily phosphatidylserine can be delivered without excessive capsule burden
- Ingredient identity and source are well controlled
- Formula cost supports sustainable pricing
- Safety profile is acceptable
- Regulatory communication territory is clear
- Formula remains simple
- B-vitamin additions have a defined need and do not dilute the product concept
- Product can be explained without exaggerated memory or disease claims

Strategic benefit:

- Stronger NervLock identity
- Better differentiation
- More coherent brain-health flagship

### Alternative Operational Sequence

**Option 2 — Brain Oil First**

Proceed when Brain Core feasibility remains unresolved but omega-3 quality and sourcing can be controlled.

Strategic benefit:

- Lower formulation complexity
- Faster establishment of quality and operational systems
- Potentially easier first manufacturing project

Risk:

- Commodity positioning
- Weak differentiation
- Greater dependence on quality transparency and education

### Not Recommended by Default

**Simultaneous launch of Brain Oil and Brain Core**

Reasons:

- Splits limited development resources
- Doubles supplier and quality complexity
- Increases content and compliance burden
- Weakens clarity about the first flagship
- Increases inventory and cash-flow risk
- Makes post-launch learning harder to interpret

A dual launch requires a separate strategic decision and a documented operational case.

## 8. Portfolio Principles

### 8.1 One Need, One Product Logic

Each product should have one primary consumer need.

Avoid products that attempt to cover:

- Memory
- Focus
- Sleep
- Energy
- Stress
- Circulation
- Mood
- Pain

within one formula.

### 8.2 Minimal Effective Formula

Every ingredient must have:

- A defined role
- Relevant evidence
- Appropriate dose
- Acceptable safety
- Manufacturability
- Stability
- Clear communication territory

Remove ingredients that exist primarily for label appeal.

### 8.3 Formula Before Marketing

Do not create a public product story before:

- Product category is known
- Formula is substantially defined
- Dose is feasible
- Evidence is reviewed
- Safety is reviewed
- Regulatory territory is understood

### 8.4 Quality as Product Strategy

Quality is not a back-office function.

For NervLock, quality is part of:

- Product identity
- Consumer trust
- Product education
- Long-term differentiation

### 8.5 No Hidden Underdosing

Do not include a meaningful ingredient at a token dose to create label appeal.

### 8.6 No Unsupported Synergy

Do not use “synergy” unless the exact combination has relevant evidence.

### 8.7 No Disease Commercialization

Disease burden may inform education.

It must not become a product indication without a lawful and scientifically defensible basis.

## 9. Product Development Stage Model

Use the following controlled stages.

### Stage 0 — Idea

A potential concept has been identified.

### Stage 1 — Strategic Screening

Brand fit, need, audience, and category are assessed.

### Stage 2 — Opportunity Assessment

Evidence, safety, regulation, market, quality, and commercial feasibility are reviewed.

### Stage 3 — Target Product Profile

The intended product is defined.

### Stage 4 — Evidence and Safety Dossier

Evidence, dose, form, population, harms, and limitations are documented.

### Stage 5 — Regulatory Pathway

Product category and communication territory are confirmed.

### Stage 6 — Supplier and Manufacturer Qualification

Candidate suppliers and manufacturers are evaluated.

### Stage 7 — Prototype Formulation

Initial formula and dosage form are developed.

### Stage 8 — Prototype Testing

Identity, potency, quality, sensory, stability, and manufacturability are assessed.

### Stage 9 — Formula Lock

The final formula is approved for validation.

### Stage 10 — Label and Claim Development

Label, warnings, permitted functions, and product-page territory are developed.

### Stage 11 — Pre-Launch Readiness

Manufacturing, inventory, support, complaint, adverse-event, recall, website, and content systems are verified.

### Stage 12 — Launch

The approved product becomes publicly available.

### Stage 13 — Post-Market Monitoring

Complaints, adverse events, quality, returns, consumer understanding, and communication accuracy are monitored.

### Stage 14 — Revision, Expansion, or Discontinuation

The product is revised, expanded, held, or discontinued based on evidence and performance.

- Regulatory category is confirmed

- Formula does not become unnecessarily complex
- Product size remains acceptable

- Target population rationale is clear
- Dose is justified
- Safety and masking issues are considered
- Public wording does not imply treatment of neurological disease

### Include Folate Only When

- Dose and form are justified
- B12 status considerations are addressed
- Product rationale is clear
- Total exposure from other products is considered

### Include Vitamin D3 Only When

- It has a defined product role
- The dose fits the intended population
- It does not distract from the core product
- Product positioning remains coherent

### Remove an Ingredient When

- It exists mainly for label appeal
- Dose is too low to matter
- Dose is difficult to justify
- It creates interaction or safety concerns
- It increases capsule burden materially
- It weakens the core product story
- It increases regulatory complexity without sufficient benefit

## 55. Current High-Priority Risks

### Risk 1 — Premature Product Identity

NervLock may be perceived as a supplement company before the educational platform is mature.

Mitigation:

- Maintain education-first website hierarchy
- Keep product content subordinate
- Avoid product-led homepage
- Continue high-quality educational publishing

### Risk 2 — Brain Core Formula Complexity

Multiple vitamins may dilute the PS-centered concept.

Mitigation:

- Require ingredient-by-ingredient inclusion rationale
- Remove nonessential ingredients
- Compare PS-only and PS-plus prototypes

### Risk 3 — Brain Core Capsule Burden

Evidence-relevant dose may require multiple capsules.

Mitigation:

- Obtain real prototype data
- Compare capsule sizes
- Assess adherence and consumer acceptance

### Risk 4 — Brain Oil Commoditization

Omega-3 may be difficult to differentiate.

Mitigation:

- Focus on verified quality, transparency, dose, oxidation, and education
- Do not rely on rTG terminology alone

### Risk 5 — Claim Inflation

Marketing may convert functional or mechanistic evidence into memory or disease claims.

Mitigation:

- Claim inventory
- Regulatory review
- Channel-specific approval
- Influencer controls
- QA checklist

### Risk 6 — Supplier Marketing Dependence

Supplier dossiers may overstate quality or evidence.

Mitigation:

- Independent verification
- Specifications
- Testing
- Reference audit
- Audit rights

### Risk 7 — Inventory and Cash-Flow Pressure

High minimum order quantities may pressure premature launch or discounting.

Mitigation:

- Conservative order size
- Realistic demand model
- No dual launch by default
- Expiration-risk analysis

A product advances because it passes a gate.

It does not pass a gate because a launch date exists.

- Need
- Evidence
- Safety
- Quality
- Transparency
- Regulatory defensibility
- Brand fit
- Operational readiness

The strongest product is not the product with:

- The most ingredients
- The highest dose
- The most aggressive claim
- The fastest launch
- The largest market

The strongest product is the one NervLock can explain honestly, control reliably, support safely, and defend over time.

Long-term trust remains more important than short-term portfolio growth.
